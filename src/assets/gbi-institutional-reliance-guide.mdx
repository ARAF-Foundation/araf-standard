This guide is addressed to institutional audiences that rely on ARAF certification and Governance Benchmark Index scores in underwriting, investment, regulatory, and counterparty decisions. It does not describe the ARAF assessment methodology. It describes how the outputs of that methodology should be interpreted and how reliance on those outputs is structured.

---

## 1. What the GBI Produces

The Governance Benchmark Index is a composite governance signal. It translates a six-dimensional assessment of an organisation's governance architecture into a form that institutional audiences can interpret without re-running the underlying methodology.

The GBI produces two outputs. The composite score expresses overall governance posture on a 1.0 to 5.0 scale where 1.0 represents the strongest governance architecture. The dimensional profile shows where governance strength and weakness sit across each of the six assessed dimensions.

Both outputs are required for institutional reliance. A composite score without a dimensional profile obscures concentrated weakness. Two deployments may produce similar composite scores while carrying very different governance risk profiles. The dimensional profile reveals what the composite score compresses.

## 2. How ARAF Evaluates Decision Governance

### 2.1 Decision Production as the Unit of Governance

ARAF evaluates governance of autonomous systems by evaluating the governance of the infrastructure through which those systems produce consequential decisions. The governance question is not whether the model is accurate or the system is technically safe. It is whether the institution deploying the system has built the governance architecture that consequential decision production requires.

Every agentic system produces outcomes through a sequence that begins with information entering the system, proceeds through the infrastructure that generates decisions, and ends with the institutional mechanisms that absorb the consequences of those decisions.

### 2.2 The Three Governance Layers

Although the ARAF methodology evaluates six independent governance dimensions, institutional audiences often find the framework easiest to understand when viewed in relation to this decision production sequence.

When interpreted through that lens, the six dimensions can be understood as governing three stages of the decision production lifecycle.

**Input Governance**

This layer governs the information environment in which the system operates. Dimension 2 evaluates the sensitivity of the data environment and the regulatory exposure created by the information the system processes. Before any consequential decision is produced, the system is already operating within a data environment that carries legal, regulatory, and reputational exposure. Input governance determines how dangerous that environment is.

**Decision Production Governance**

This layer governs the infrastructure that produces consequential decisions. Dimension 1 evaluates the degree of autonomous authority exercised by the system when producing outcomes. Dimension 6 evaluates whether governance mechanisms remain stable as the system evolves.

Autonomy determines the volume and velocity of consequential decisions generated by the system, which is why Dimension 1 functions as the force multiplier across the framework. Adaptive stability determines whether the governance architecture assessed at certification continues to reflect the system as it operates during the reliance period.

**Institutional Governance**

This layer governs the institutional structures surrounding the system that allocate responsibility for the outcomes it produces. Dimension 3 evaluates the contractual infrastructure governing the system. Dimension 4 evaluates the organisation's liability architecture for autonomous action consequences. Dimension 5 evaluates whether the organisation retains the practical commercial leverage necessary to remediate governance failures. Dimension 5 governs the organisation's capacity to restore governance rather than the consequences of any individual decision.

### 2.3 Relationship to the Six Dimensions

This interpretive structure does not alter the ARAF methodology. Dimensional scoring and multiplier logic remain unchanged. The three-layer model provides a way to understand how the dimensions collectively evaluate governance across the lifecycle of decision production. It does not create additional scoring criteria or modify certification thresholds.

### 2.4 Why Multiplier Conditions Occur

The GBI includes multiplier logic because governance weaknesses compound rather than simply accumulate. The three-layer structure explains why.

The Systemic Escalation condition (Dimension 1 and Dimension 4) occurs when high autonomous decision velocity in the decision production layer combines with inadequate liability architecture in the institutional layer. The system produces consequential outcomes at scale without the institutional structure to manage them.

The Infrastructure Collapse condition (Dimension 3 and Dimension 1) occurs when significant autonomy in the decision production layer combines with inadequate contractual infrastructure in the institutional layer. Liability allocation at the execution boundary is ungoverned.

The Leverage Collapse condition (Dimension 5 and Dimension 4) occurs when high commercial dependency in the institutional layer combines with inadequate liability architecture. The organisation is structurally resistant to remediation precisely when remediation is required.

Each multiplier condition is a cross-layer governance failure. The dimensional profile identifies which layer is failing and in which combination.

## 3. Certification Tiers

ARAF certification tiers translate GBI scores into institutional signals that external parties can act on.

**ARAF Assessed** indicates that governance posture has been independently evaluated and is available for institutional review. No minimum score is required. This is the entry tier for institutional engagement.

**ARAF Compliant** requires a GBI score of 2.50 or below and a Governance Coherence Index of 0.70 or above across all assessed dimensions. It represents the minimum institutional threshold: governance posture adequate for reliance in most deployment contexts. Analogous to SOC 2 Type II in cybersecurity governance.

**ARAF Certified** requires a GBI score of 1.75 or below and a GCI of 0.85 or above across all assessed dimensions, with Tier 1 evidence for at least 80% of sampled controls. It represents full agentic bankability: governance posture that supports classification, insurance, financing, and institutional reliance.

The tiers create a governance progression that institutions can track over time. An insurer can price conditional coverage during a remediation period and transition to standard terms on Compliant certification. An investor can model the governance discount at the current score and the valuation uplift on reaching the next threshold.

## 4. Governance Benchmark Index Interpretation

The interpretation of Governance Benchmark Index score ranges is defined in the GBI Methodology section of the ARAF standard. That section contains the canonical scale interpretation table, which specifies the governance meaning of each score range.

Institutional users should reference the GBI Methodology when interpreting the governance signal associated with a given GBI score. This guide does not reproduce that table in order to preserve a single authoritative interpretation of the GBI scale within the standard.

### Institutional Reliance Context

The Governance Benchmark Index expresses the structural strength of the governance architecture surrounding autonomous decision systems. GBI values provide a governance architecture signal, not a prediction of operational outcomes or loss probability.

Institutional actors — including insurers, regulators, investors, and boards — may incorporate GBI signals as part of broader risk assessment processes when evaluating the governance reliability of autonomous system deployments. Interpretation of GBI values should be read together with the GBI Methodology, which defines score construction and scale interpretation; the Certification Framework, which defines assessment outcomes and certification validity; and the Governance Coherence Index, which provides additional governance stability signals where applicable.

ARAF does not prescribe underwriting decisions or regulatory outcomes. Institutional reliance decisions remain the responsibility of the relying party.

### The Underwriting Problem ARAF Addresses

Without a structured governance signal, insurers face an adverse selection problem when underwriting autonomous system deployments. They cannot distinguish between systems whose governance posture warrants coverage and systems whose governance posture does not. All deployments present as comparable because there is no comparable governance signal.

The GBI addresses this directly. It allows underwriters to evaluate governance architecture around consequential decision infrastructure in structured, comparable form. This is the function the framework performs for insurance markets: not determining insurability, but providing the governance information that makes underwriting judgment possible.

Multiplier conditions, where present, indicate compounding governance exposure across governance layers. An assessment report carrying a multiplier condition requires dimensional analysis before underwriting assumptions are applied.

## 5. Certification Validity and Reliance Periods

### Certification Validity

ARAF certification reflects the governance posture of a system at the time of assessment. Certification remains formally valid for the period specified by tier, unless revoked or superseded by a subsequent assessment.

| Certification Tier | Validity Period |
|---|---|
| ARAF Assessed | 60 days from report issue |
| ARAF Compliant | 12 months from certification date |
| ARAF Certified | 24 months from certification date, with mandatory 12-month interim surveillance assessment |

### Reliance Freshness

External reliance on certification assumes that the assessed system has not undergone material change since the assessment.

For this reason, institutional reliance periods are shorter than certification validity periods. Unless otherwise specified in the assessment report, certification may be relied upon by insurers, regulators, or counterparties for a period of 60 days from the date of report issue without additional verification of system state.

After this period, reliance requires confirmation that no material system changes have occurred since the assessment.

<Aside type="note" title="GCA Addendum §7">
The 60-day reliance freshness period supersedes the 30-day figure that appears in earlier versions of the Governance Coherence Addendum. The canonical reliance freshness window is 60 days from report issue.
</Aside>

### Material Change

Material changes include modifications that may affect autonomy, governance stability, contractual structure, liability architecture, or the organisation's capacity to remediate governance failures.

Examples include model replacement or retraining that affects decision thresholds, introduction of new automated execution pathways, integration with additional external systems, significant change to operational scope, or a material incident in which the governance architecture was engaged or found insufficient.

Where material change occurs, reassessment may be required before certification can be relied upon. Organisations must notify their accredited assessor of triggering events within 60 days of occurrence.

### Assessment Window

The minimum assessment window for Governance Coherence evaluation at the Compliant and Certified tiers is 180 days. The assessment window must be specified in the certification report. Institutional reliance should be calibrated to the assessment window stated in the report: a certification covering a longer assessment window provides a more robust governance signal than one covering the minimum period.

## 6. Evidence Quality and Reliance

The confidence warranted in a GBI score depends not only on the score itself but on the evidence quality tier supporting it.

Tier 1 evidence is produced contemporaneously by governance infrastructure as a natural output of governance operations. It cannot be retrospectively assembled. An assessment supported by Tier 1 evidence provides the highest confidence that the governance posture reflected in the GBI score was operationally real during the assessment window.

Tier 2 evidence is produced at the time of governance decisions but not by automated infrastructure. It provides high confidence for documented decisions where documentation practices are consistent and complete.

Tier 3 evidence is assembled retrospectively. It is acceptable only for the Assessed tier and does not support Compliant or Certified certification.

Institutional reliance should account for the evidence tier. An insurer relying on an assessment supported by Tier 1 evidence is working from a qualitatively different evidence base than one relying on the minimum evidence standard.

---

## 7. Reliance Boundaries

ARAF certification provides a governance signal. It does not provide the following.

Certification is not a guarantee of future system performance, safety, or absence of adverse outcomes. Governance architecture reduces the probability and severity of governance failure. It does not eliminate it.

Certification is not a finding that the organisation is compliant with any specific regulatory requirement. The GBI dimensional profile maps to requirements under the EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, APRA CPS 230, and the Corporations Act section 180(1) duty of care standard, but certification does not constitute legal compliance with any of those frameworks.

Certification is not a substitute for institutional due diligence. Boards, insurers, investors, and regulators retain responsibility for their own governance judgments. The GBI informs those judgments; it does not replace them.
