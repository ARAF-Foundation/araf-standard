## 2. Distributed Decision Infrastructure

Modern organisations increasingly produce consequential decisions through Distributed Decision Infrastructure (DDI).

Distributed Decision Infrastructure refers to the network of autonomous systems, human experts, data infrastructure, and external service providers through which consequential decisions are produced.

A single operational decision in an AI-enabled organisation may involve:

- an autonomous system generating a recommendation
- an offshore analyst reviewing the output
- a fractional executive approving the action
- a cloud infrastructure provider executing the transaction

Each participant operates under a different contractual relationship, often in different jurisdictions.

The decision is produced collectively by this infrastructure rather than by a single identifiable actor.

Governance structures designed for hierarchical, human-authored decision making do not govern this infrastructure.

## 3. The Decision Supply Chain

The Decision Supply Chain is the sequence of systems, actors, and processes through which a consequential decision is produced.

It extends from:

- the data inputs informing the system
- through the autonomous systems and human actors shaping the decision
- to the infrastructure that executes the decision.

The Decision Supply Chain captures the process through which a decision is produced, while Distributed Decision Infrastructure describes the network of participants involved in that process.

Together they constitute the organisation's decision infrastructure.

Governance of autonomous systems is therefore governance of decision infrastructure.

### Automated Application Generation and Misrepresentation Risk

Insurance application formation now frequently occurs through distributed decision infrastructure rather than a single human author.

In a typical deployment, the application record may be produced through the following chain:

- foundation model provider
- document extraction system
- brokerage platform
- autofill software
- consumer interface
- underwriting ingestion system

The insurer relies on the submitted application to issue coverage. A dispute then arises after a claim because a material field was inferred incorrectly, extracted inaccurately, or autofilled from the wrong source.

Traditional accountability allocation fails in this scenario. The consumer did not author the field. The broker did not directly generate the field. The insurer relied on the field. The software chain generated the field.

Without chain-complete governance records, the accountability question becomes legally indeterminate. Misrepresentation analysis, E and O attachment, and formation validity all depend on whether the decision infrastructure can demonstrate how the application artefact was produced.

This is why ARAF governs the full Decision Supply Chain rather than only the model at the center.

Each ARAF dimension addresses a governance requirement at a specific stage of the Decision Supply Chain: the distributed chain of data, models, operators, and execution systems through which consequential decisions are produced.

The dimensions assess governance at each stage of the chain. Technical safety standards assess whether the system operates correctly at each stage. Both are necessary. ARAF addresses whether the institution deploying the system can demonstrate accountability for the decisions the chain produces.

## 4. The Foundational Principle: Reconstructability

The ARAF Standard is built on a foundational institutional principle:

Institutions can rely on autonomous systems only to the extent that the decisions those systems produce can be reconstructed from contemporaneous governance records.

This requirement applies not only to the autonomous system at the centre of the chain but to every link through which the decision travels.


## 5. Why the Chain Matters for Accountability

Institutional accountability ultimately resolves to three questions:


These questions must be answerable for every link in the Decision Supply Chain, not only for the autonomous system itself.

Regulators, insurers, and investors will not ask only whether a system functioned correctly.

They will ask:

- who controlled the system
- where decisions were made
- under which jurisdictional authority decisions occurred
- what each participant in the chain knew and did.

A decision produced by distributed infrastructure therefore has a chain of accountability, not a single point of accountability.
## 6. The Chain in Practice

![ARAF Decision Supply Chain architecture: four layers (Data, Model, Human Review, Execution) with three governance boundaries](/images/araf/araf_dsc_architecture.svg)

Governance must exist outside the model, at the architectural boundaries through which model inputs and outputs flow.

Four boundaries define the operational structure of the Decision Supply Chain:

**Data Boundary**  
Governs the information entering the system.

**Inference Boundary**  
Governs how models interact with inputs and produce outputs.

**Action Boundary**  
Governs the consequential actions the system is permitted to trigger.

		## Governance Across the Decision Path

		ARAF assesses governance across the full decision path rather than at a single control point. A governance architecture that addresses only one stage does not govern the decision path.

		For interpretive purposes, the decision path comprises five governance layers:

		**Stage 01 — Admissibility.** Whether the request, state, provenance, authority, and context are eligible to enter the governed system. Admissibility is assessed before model invocation or equivalent decision initiation. Its absence is a governance failure at the earliest stage of the path. Maps to: data boundary. Assessed under D2.

		**Stage 02 — Decision Formation.** Whether the system's emerging decision is being formed within defined governance conditions, escalation thresholds, and intervention rules. The question is not only whether the system has authority to act, but whether the decision being formed is appropriate for the specific task and circumstances at that moment. Maps to: inference boundary. Assessed under D1.
 The concept of [decision integrity](/standard/decision-integrity/) is central to this stage.
		**Stage 03 — Action Boundary.** Whether the proposed output is permitted to cross into operational consequence. Authorisation to act is not the same as admissibility to commit. Maps to: action boundary. Assessed under D1 and D3.

		**Stage 04 — Traceability.** Whether the path from input to consequence is reconstructable through reliable, contemporaneous evidence. Governance that only evaluates after the decision has propagated is not governance. It is documentation of what already happened. Maps to: evidence architecture. Assessed across all dimensions.

		**Stage 05 — Post-Deployment Monitoring.** Whether the organisation monitors behavioural drift, recurring failure patterns, governance degradation, and cross-system signals over time. Where multiple governed systems operate in parallel, monitoring must extend beyond single-system review to include portfolio-level signals. Maps to: lifecycle layer. Assessed under D6.

		ARAF does not prescribe how decisions are shaped in-flight. It defines the governance and evidentiary conditions under which those decisions are institutionally defensible.

Each boundary corresponds to a layer of the Decision Supply Chain.

### Autonomous System Layer

The autonomous system is typically the most visible component of the chain.

Classification, monitoring, and evidence programs often focus on the model itself. This focus is necessary but insufficient because the system operates within a broader decision infrastructure.

### Data Infrastructure Layer

The data infrastructure supplying the system introduces critical governance questions including:

- training data provenance
- intellectual property rights
- consent frameworks where personal data is involved.

Training data exposure is backward-compounding. Exposure is created at the moment of training but persists across the entire operational life of the system.

Every decision produced by the system reflects the data on which the system was trained.

### Human Review Layer

Many deployments incorporate a human review stage.

Examples include:

- offshore analysts
- fractional executives
- internal approval functions.

These actors are links in the Decision Supply Chain.

Their accountability must be:

- assigned by role
- supported by appropriate contractual structures
- incorporated into the governance evidence record.

Where reviewers are licensed professionals such as lawyers, doctors, or financial advisers, professional licensing obligations operate alongside organisational governance obligations.

Both frameworks must be satisfied.

A governance record that documents model outputs but not the human review stage does not satisfy the accountability requirement.

### Execution Infrastructure Layer

The infrastructure executing system decisions is frequently operated by third parties.

Examples include:

- cloud infrastructure providers
- payment processors
- data exchange platforms.

Where execution infrastructure operates in another jurisdiction, additional contractual and governance complexity arises.

A critical distinction applies:

Commodity infrastructure providers require different governance treatment than decision-participating providers such as model developers or retrieval systems whose outputs influence the content of decisions.

Governance obligations should be proportional to the provider's influence on the decision outcome.

### The Jurisdictional Boundary

The jurisdictional boundary governs where the decision's consequences take legal effect. It is the governance point for cross-border liability, regulatory compliance, and provider designation risk. Agentic AI deployments routinely cross jurisdictional boundaries without deliberate organisational decision: the vendor's foundation model may operate under US law; customer data may be subject to GDPR, the Australian Privacy Act, or equivalent national privacy frameworks; enterprise customers may operate across multiple jurisdictions; and the agent may execute actions inside those environments without regard for borders.

The jurisdictional boundary requires governance treatment distinct from the data, inference, and action boundaries. A system that is correctly governed at the action boundary, with functioning scope enforcement, may still produce ungoverned consequences at the jurisdictional boundary where the action takes legal effect in a jurisdiction where the governance architecture does not apply. D3 Contract Infrastructure assesses the contractual layer at this boundary. The Decision Supply Chain map must identify each jurisdictional boundary crossed in the chain and the governance treatment applied at each crossing.

For detailed jurisdictional footprint mapping procedures, cross-border liability architecture requirements, and regulatory exposure mapping methodology, see [ARAF Assessor Guidance Note GN-002: Cross-Border Deployment Assessment](/assessor-guidance/gn-002/).

## 7. Governance Requirements for Each Link

### Chain Mapping

Organisations must maintain a documented map of each Decision Supply Chain.

This map should identify:

- each system involved and its ARAF classification
- each human actor participating in the chain
- the contractual relationships governing each link
- the jurisdictions through which the chain operates.

Chain mapping must be maintained continuously as systems evolve and providers change.

### Evidence Continuity

![ARAF Decision Supply Chain evidence continuity: chain-complete versus chain-incomplete evidence records](/images/araf/araf_dsc_evidence_continuity.svg)

Evidence continuity across the chain is the operational mechanism through which reconstructability is achieved.

Governance records must follow the decision through the entire chain.

Incomplete evidence records, for example records documenting model outputs but not the human approval stage, do not satisfy governance requirements.

## 8. The Bilateral Governance Principle

The reconstructability requirement is bilateral.

Institutions assessing governance standards in others must meet equivalent governance standards in their own decision infrastructure.

For example, an insurer evaluating an organisation's AI governance must itself maintain reconstructable governance records if it deploys autonomous systems in underwriting or claims processing.

An institution cannot credibly evaluate governance adequacy in others unless its own decision infrastructure satisfies the same reconstructability requirements.

This principle ensures institutional coherence in governance assessment.

## 9. The Jurisdictional Dimension

Decision Supply Chains frequently span multiple jurisdictions.

The deploying organisation remains accountable for the chain's outcomes regardless of where individual links operate.

Contractual arrangements may allocate liability among participants, but they do not eliminate the deploying organisation's ultimate responsibility for decisions produced by the chain.

Jurisdictional complexity therefore multiplies governance risk rather than distributing accountability.

## 10. Why This Gap Persists

Organisations frequently govern the autonomous system itself but fail to govern the broader chain.

Three factors explain this pattern.

### Visibility

The model is visible technology. Human reviewers and contractual relationships are organisational arrangements. Governance attention follows technological visibility.

### Contractual inertia

Many service agreements were drafted before the Decision Supply Chain concept existed. Existing contracts often fail to allocate governance responsibilities appropriately.

### Assumption of coverage

Organisations frequently assume existing insurance policies cover autonomous action risk. In practice, liability exposure often falls between policy categories because the chain producing the decision was never governed as a chain.

This is the underwriting uncertainty ARAF is designed to remove: AI risk becomes insurable only when governance infrastructure produces comparable evidence of decision accountability.

## 11. Governance-Based Insurability

Within the ARAF Standard, Governance-Based Insurability (GBI) functions as the compressed signal of reconstructability.

GBI translates detailed governance assessments, including evidence architecture, accountability structures, and chain mapping, into a signal that insurers, investors, and regulators can rely upon.

The signal communicates that the organisation's decision infrastructure produces reconstructable decisions supported by contemporaneous governance records.

GBI therefore allows institutional counterparties to assess governance reliability without reproducing the full governance assessment themselves.

## 12. Certification

Certification provides institutional verification that the reconstructability requirement has been satisfied.

Certification confirms that governance records exist that allow the three accountability questions to be answered from contemporaneous records:

- who was responsible
- what they knew
- what actions they took.

External institutions rely on certification as evidence that the decision infrastructure producing autonomous system outcomes is governable and accountable.

The institutional consequence of inadequate evidence architecture is concrete. Consider an insurer that issues liability coverage for an autonomous system deployment based on representations about governance adequacy. When a claim arises from an adverse decision outcome, the insurer attempts to reconstruct the decision chain that produced the liability event only to discover that the organisation cannot produce contemporaneous governance records sufficient to demonstrate what the system processed, what human reviewers evaluated, or what approval processes occurred. Coverage scope becomes uncertain because the formation record cannot be reconstructed. Both insurer and insured face unallocated exposure not because the system malfunctioned but because the decision that produced the outcome cannot be reconstructed from contemporaneous records.

## 13. Implementation Principles

Three principles apply to Decision Supply Chain governance regardless of organisational stage.

### Govern the chain, not just the system

The autonomous system is one governance object. The Decision Supply Chain is a broader governance object that must also be governed.

### Contracts must reflect function

Contracts governing each link should reflect the governance role that link performs in the decision infrastructure.

### Evidence must be chain-complete

The governance record is complete only when it documents every link through which the decision travelled.

Constructing these records during system deployment is substantially less costly than reconstructing them after an adverse event.

## 14. The Purpose of the Standard

The ARAF Standard exists to serve a foundational institutional proposition:

Institutions can rely on autonomous systems only to the extent that the decisions those systems produce can be reconstructed from contemporaneous governance records.

Every classification method, accountability structure, and evidence requirement within the Standard operationalises this proposition.

The Standard provides the governance architecture through which organisations can demonstrate that their decision infrastructure produces reconstructable decisions.

Reconstructability is therefore the organising principle from which the governance obligations of the ARAF Standard flow.
