# GN-002: Cross-Border Deployment Assessment

*Assessor Guidance Note GN-002 · Agentic Risk Architecture Framework v3.0*

---

This guidance note provides supplementary methodology for assessors conducting ARAF governance assessments where the system under assessment operates across more than one jurisdiction. It extends GN-001 and must be applied in conjunction with it, not as a standalone document.

Where GN-001 is the foundational practice guide, GN-002 addresses the specific compound complexity that cross-border deployment introduces. The assessor applies GN-001 throughout and layers GN-002 on top from Section 2 onwards. Any GN-001 procedure that does not have a cross-border extension in GN-002 applies without modification.

This guidance note must be read in conjunction with the [Assessor Accreditation Standard](/assessor-guidance/assessor-accreditation-standard/), [GN-001: Conducting an ARAF Governance Assessment](/assessor-guidance/gn-001/), and the normative specification documents referenced in Section 9.

## 1. The Cross-Border Assessment Problem

Cross-border deployment is not simply more of the same assessment work. It creates a structural problem that a single-jurisdiction assessment methodology cannot address.

The structural problem is extraterritorial regulatory reach. The most demanding jurisdictional requirement in the deployment footprint effectively sets the standard for the entire system. The EU AI Act applies to any system affecting EU residents regardless of where the deploying organisation is incorporated. The Australian Privacy Act applies to systems processing data of Australian residents regardless of where that processing occurs. Regulatory frameworks are moving from domestic to extraterritorial in scope, from voluntary to mandatory status, and from principle-based to specification-based requirements. The assessor must evaluate the deployment against the trajectory of those frameworks, not merely their current state.

The core GN-002 principle is this: a cross-border deployment cannot be governed by a single jurisdiction's standards. The assessment scope must encompass every jurisdiction in which the system operates, processes data, produces decisions, or creates legal consequences.

This principle has a direct implication for scope definition. Before commencing dimensional scoring, the assessor must establish the complete jurisdictional footprint of the deployment. That footprint is the scope within which the assessment operates.

---

## 2. Jurisdictional Footprint Mapping

Jurisdictional footprint mapping is the foundational procedure for every cross-border assessment. It must be completed before dimensional scoring begins. It is an extension of the Decision Supply Chain mapping required by GN-001 Section 2, with a jurisdictional layer added at each chain link.

### The jurisdiction matrix

For each link in the Decision Supply Chain, the assessor must identify:

- the jurisdiction in which the link operates
- the jurisdiction whose law governs the contractual relationship at that link
- the jurisdictions in which the data processed at that link originated
- the jurisdictions in which the decisions produced at that link create legal consequences
- the jurisdictions in which the system's outputs are consumed by end users or downstream systems

These will frequently not be the same jurisdiction. A system may be built in one jurisdiction, trained on data from multiple jurisdictions, deployed by an operator in another jurisdiction, and produce decisions affecting residents in a further set of jurisdictions. Each of those facts carries distinct regulatory obligations.

The minimum output from this procedure is a jurisdiction matrix cross-referencing each chain link against each applicable regulatory framework. This matrix is the foundation document for the rest of the cross-border assessment. It must be included in the assessment report as a standalone exhibit.

### Regulatory framework identification

For each jurisdiction identified in the footprint, the assessor must identify the primary regulatory frameworks governing the system's operation in that jurisdiction. This includes AI-specific frameworks, data protection frameworks, sector-specific requirements, and liability frameworks that apply to autonomous system deployments.

The three regulatory trajectories that inform the assessor's analysis are: voluntary to mandatory status; principle-based to specification-based requirements; and domestic to extraterritorial scope. The assessor must note, for each applicable framework, where it currently sits on each trajectory and what governance investment that trajectory implies within the assessment horizon.

## 3. Dimensional Scoring Adjustments for Cross-Border Deployment

Cross-border deployment creates simultaneous compound problems across four of the six dimensions. Each dimension requires specific cross-border guidance. A dimension that scores adequately in a single-jurisdiction assessment may score materially differently once the full jurisdictional footprint is mapped.

### D1 — Autonomy Gradient

Jurisdictional accountability for AI inference is established at the inference boundary. In cross-border deployments, the jurisdiction that governs accountability for a given decision may depend on where the inference occurs, where the decision is implemented, and where the affected party is located.

The assessor must identify which jurisdiction's accountability standard applies to each material decision type and assess whether the governance architecture reflects those standards. A supervisory oversight structure adequate for one jurisdiction's human oversight requirements may be inadequate under another jurisdiction's requirements for the same system.

Score the effective autonomy level against the most demanding applicable standard in the deployment footprint. Document the jurisdictional basis for the score.

### D2 — Data Sensitivity Exposure

Cross-border transfer restrictions create direct scoring implications. The D2 assessment must address operational data sensitivity separately in each jurisdiction — different categories of data attract regulatory protection in different jurisdictions — and must address training data provenance across the full jurisdictional footprint.

The jurisdictional divergence on training data memorisation is analytically significant. Different jurisdictions have reached different conclusions on whether model memorisation of training data constitutes ongoing reproduction for copyright purposes. A single provenance governance position may be insufficient across the deployment footprint. The assessor must evaluate whether the organisation has jurisdiction-specific provenance documentation, or a documentation approach that is assessable against the most demanding applicable standard.

Cross-border data transfer mechanisms — adequacy decisions, standard contractual clauses, binding corporate rules, and equivalent instruments — must each be assessed for adequacy given the system's actual data flows. The existence of a transfer mechanism does not establish its adequacy; the assessor must evaluate whether the mechanism is appropriate for the data categories transferred and the jurisdictions involved.

### D3 — Contract Infrastructure

Contract infrastructure in cross-border deployments must address: which jurisdiction's law governs each contract; which jurisdiction has enforcement jurisdiction over disputes arising from the system's operation; and whether force majeure provisions adequately address jurisdictional events in each relevant jurisdiction.

Jurisdictional events include government designation events, regulatory interventions, data localisation mandates, and sector-specific prohibitions that could affect the system's operation or a provider's ability to perform. The assessor must evaluate whether the force majeure provisions in each material contract address the specific jurisdictional event types relevant to the deployment footprint — not merely the generic force majeure language that contract infrastructure typically contains.

The Political Cascade multiplier is particularly relevant in cross-border deployments. Political Cascade is most likely to trigger where government-adjacent customer concentration intersects with inadequate political force majeure provisions governing designation events in one or more jurisdictions. Multi-jurisdictional government exposure is a mandatory Political Cascade screening item in every cross-border assessment. Document the screening determination explicitly, whether or not the multiplier is triggered.

### D4 — Liability Architecture

Cross-border deployment creates overlapping and potentially contradictory liability frameworks. The assessor must evaluate whether the organisation's liability architecture reflects the most demanding applicable framework across the deployment footprint, not merely the framework of its home jurisdiction.

Where the deployment footprint includes EU-adjacent operations, the EU Product Liability Directive creates lifecycle strict liability: the system can become defective through retraining or self-learning after market placement. This means each retraining cycle is a potential new product defect event, creating a reassessment trigger obligation that operates independently of the ARAF reassessment trigger regime. The assessor must document whether the organisation's liability architecture reflects this and whether the retraining governance protocol addresses it.

Liability caps structured for a single jurisdiction may be inadequate when cross-border adverse outcomes create multi-jurisdictional litigation exposure. Assess whether the liability caps in each material agreement are adequate for the combined cross-border exposure the system creates, not merely the single-jurisdiction exposure the agreement was drafted to address.

### D5 — Commercial Leverage

Cross-border deployment creates provider dependency risks that are invisible in single-jurisdiction assessments. Where a critical provider is subject to regulatory designation risk in a jurisdiction that is also a material customer jurisdiction — the most acute form being government-adjacent operations in jurisdictions where provider designation is a policy instrument — the concentration risk is compounded.

The assessor must evaluate whether the organisation has provider substitution capacity that is realistic given the regulatory environment in each deployment jurisdiction, not merely technically feasible in the abstract. A substitution that is operationally viable in one jurisdiction may be regulatory-constrained in another. The practical substitutability assessment must account for jurisdictional constraints on substitution, not merely technical and commercial ones.

### D6 — Adaptive Stability

Governance drift detection must function across all jurisdictions in the deployment footprint. A monitoring framework calibrated to one jurisdiction's regulatory requirements will miss drift signals generated by changing requirements in other jurisdictions.

The assessor must evaluate whether the organisation's governance monitoring infrastructure tracks regulatory development across all deployment jurisdictions, not merely its home jurisdiction. The trajectory from principle-based to specification-based requirements means that governance adequacy against current principles may become inadequate as those principles are specified into rules. The D6 assessment must include an evaluation of whether the organisation's governance monitoring is tracking that trajectory in each applicable jurisdiction and whether an adaptation plan exists.

## 4. Regulatory Exposure Mapping

The assessor must produce a regulatory exposure map as a standalone assessment output, separate from the dimensional scoring. The map identifies, for each jurisdiction in the deployment footprint:

- the primary regulatory frameworks governing the system's operation
- the current status of each framework: voluntary, mandatory, or transitional
- the trajectory of each framework across the three dimensions: voluntary to mandatory, principle to specificity, domestic to extraterritorial
- the organisation's documented alignment with each framework
- the governance investment the framework trajectory implies within the assessment horizon

The regulatory exposure map is both an assessment tool and a required report component. It translates the abstract risk of regulatory change into specific governance investment requirements that boards, insurers, and investors can evaluate.

Where the organisation has active regulatory sandbox participation in a deployment jurisdiction, this is a D3 and D4 strengthening factor. Sandbox participation represents demonstrated governance adequacy to that jurisdiction's regulator under supervised conditions. The assessor should recognise this explicitly and document its basis in the scoring rationale.

## 5. Tax and Commercial Structuring Considerations

This section is scoped narrowly. GN-002 is an assessor guidance note, not a tax advisory document. The assessor's role is to identify whether the organisation's entity structure creates governance gaps, not to advise on optimal tax structuring.

The governance question is whether there is misalignment between the entity that operates the system and the entity that bears liability for its decisions. In cross-border autonomous system deployments, the entity that sells the product, the entity that bears contractual liability, the entity that employs the governance personnel, and the entity that owns the intellectual property may be different entities in different jurisdictions. That misalignment creates D3 and D4 governance gaps that the assessor must identify.

The specific flags the assessor should document are: intellectual property held in a jurisdiction different from the jurisdiction of deployment operation; liability structure that routes claims through an entity without adequate assets to satisfy them; and employment of governance personnel in a jurisdiction different from the jurisdiction of operational accountability.

The assessor does not resolve these issues. The assessor documents them as governance gaps requiring legal and tax advice, with implications for D3 and D4 scoring, and includes them in the remediation roadmap.

## 6. Brand and Reputational Architecture Assessment

The governance question is whether the organisation has assessed and documented the reputational consequences of adverse outcomes in each deployment jurisdiction and whether that assessment has informed the governance architecture.

Cross-border deployments create reputational exposure that is not symmetrical across jurisdictions. An adverse outcome in a government-adjacent deployment in a jurisdiction with high media visibility may have reputational consequences in other deployment jurisdictions even where the legal exposure is contained. Incident response architecture that addresses the legal accountability requirements in the jurisdiction where an incident occurred may leave multi-jurisdictional reputational exposure unaddressed.

The assessor must evaluate whether the organisation's incident response architecture addresses multi-jurisdictional reputational exposure. This maps primarily to D5 (Commercial Leverage) and D6 (Adaptive Stability). A governance architecture that addresses legal accountability but not reputational architecture in a multi-jurisdictional deployment has an incomplete D6 adaptive response capability.

## 7. Cross-Border Evidence Continuity

Evidence continuity across the Decision Supply Chain becomes significantly more complex in cross-border deployments. Evidence generated in one jurisdiction may not be admissible, transferable, or retainable in another.

The assessor must evaluate whether the organisation's evidence architecture addresses three distinct problems. First, data sovereignty requirements that restrict where governance evidence can be stored — evidence that is legally required to remain in one jurisdiction may not be available for proceedings in another. Second, admissibility requirements that affect whether evidence generated in one jurisdiction is usable in proceedings in another — the standards for authentic and admissible evidence differ across jurisdictions. Third, retention requirements that vary across jurisdictions, creating the risk that evidence adequate for one jurisdiction's requirements is deficient for another's.

Where evidence continuity gaps are identified, they must be documented in the evidence summary section of the assessment report and reflected in the dimensional scoring where they create governance exposure. An organisation that cannot produce an adequate cross-jurisdictional evidence record has a D2 and D3 gap regardless of how well its single-jurisdiction evidence practice is maintained.

## 8. Report Requirements for Cross-Border Assessments

The standard assessment report format from GN-001 applies, with the following additions required for every cross-border assessment.

**Jurisdiction matrix** — the output of the jurisdictional footprint mapping in Section 2, cross-referencing each Decision Supply Chain link against each applicable regulatory framework. This is a required standalone exhibit, not a narrative summary.

**Regulatory exposure map** — the output of the regulatory exposure mapping in Section 4, identifying framework status, trajectory, and governance investment implications for each applicable jurisdiction.

**Cross-border multiplier analysis** — the standard multiplier analysis from GN-001, with an explicit notation confirming that Political Cascade has been screened for multi-jurisdictional government-adjacent customer concentration and stating the basis for the screening determination.

**Jurisdiction-specific remediation roadmap** — where governance gaps are identified in specific jurisdictions, the remediation roadmap must distinguish between gaps that affect the entire deployment and gaps that are jurisdiction-specific. Jurisdiction-specific remediation actions must identify the jurisdiction, the gap, the responsible party, and the estimated timeline.

For Compliant and Certified reports, the design adequacy findings and operational coherence findings must be separated explicitly across the jurisdictional footprint, not merely in aggregate. An institutional reader — particularly an underwriter writing cross-border coverage — needs to understand whether a governance finding is uniform across jurisdictions or whether it reflects jurisdiction-specific exposure.
