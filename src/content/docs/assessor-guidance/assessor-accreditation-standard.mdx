Accreditation requirements, independence obligations, and quality assurance for ARAF assessors.

**Version 3.0 · March 2026 · Institute for Autonomous Governance Pty Ltd**

---

## 1. Purpose and Scope

This Standard defines the requirements for accreditation as an ARAF assessor. It specifies who may conduct ARAF assessments, under what independence obligations, and subject to what quality assurance.

:::note[Normative requirement]
Only assessors accredited under this Standard may conduct assessments that produce ARAF Assessed, ARAF Compliant, or ARAF Certified designations. Assessments conducted by non-accredited parties are not ARAF assessments regardless of whether they apply the published methodology.
:::

An accredited ARAF assessor is authorised to: conduct ARAF governance assessments; evaluate governance evidence according to the ARAF Evidence Infrastructure Standard (EIS-01); apply the six-dimensional governance framework; classify evidence quality tier per dimension; calculate Governance Benchmark Index and Governance Coherence Index scores; determine multiplier conditions; and issue certification designations where thresholds are met.

The assessor's role is to evaluate governance architecture. It does not include responsibility for system design, technical development, or operational management. Assessors evaluate governance posture based on evidence produced by the organisation under assessment.

This Standard is referenced normatively in ARAF Standard v3.0, Clause 2 and Clause 11.

:::note[Design reference]
The accreditation architecture was designed with specific reference to the failure mode documented in the FTC's 2014 action against TRUSTe, Inc. (In the Matter of True Ultimate Standards Everywhere, Inc., FTC File No. 132 3219, 2014), where inadequate assessor oversight produced over 1,000 privacy seal certifications that did not reflect actual practice. ARAF addresses that failure directly: assessors are subject to accountability review, and accreditation is contingent on maintaining assessment quality.
:::

## 2. Qualification Requirements

Assessors must demonstrate competence across three domains. The domains reflect that ARAF assessment requires governance judgement, not only methodology application. An assessor who understands the scoring formula but not institutional accountability structures cannot produce an assessment that satisfies the institutional audiences the certification serves.

### 2.1 Governance Assessment Competence

Assessors must demonstrate understanding of corporate governance frameworks, board accountability standards, risk management systems, and legal accountability structures, including the standard of care applied by courts, the accountability requirements applied by regulators, and the governance expectations of insurers and investors. This competence ensures assessors can evaluate governance architecture rather than technical system behaviour alone.

### 2.2 Autonomous Systems Governance Literacy

Assessors must demonstrate familiarity with autonomous system deployment models, including Distributed Decision Infrastructure and the Decision Supply Chain; AI governance principles and emerging regulatory frameworks; model lifecycle management, including training, fine-tuning, retrieval-augmented generation, and versioning; and the distinction between performance monitoring and governance monitoring.

Technical expertise in model development is not required. Assessors must understand the operational context in which autonomous systems operate and the governance implications of deployment decisions.

### 2.3 ARAF Methodology Proficiency

Assessors must demonstrate proficiency in the six-dimensional ARAF governance framework, dimensional evidence evaluation, multiplier logic and compound weakness identification, GBI calculation, GCI methodology, evidence tier classification, and certification threshold interpretation.

Proficiency is demonstrated through completion of the ARAF Assessor Accreditation Programme, administered by the Institute for Autonomous Governance. The detailed competency requirements under each domain and the examination structure are specified in the Assessor Qualification Framework (forthcoming following completion of the pilot assessment programme).

## 3. Accreditation Levels

Accreditation is granted at one of two levels.

| Accreditation Level | Assessment Scope | Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| Level 1: Design Adequacy | ARAF Assessed tier only | Core methodology examination completed. |
| Level 2: Full Certification | All tiers (Assessed, Compliant, Certified) | Core methodology examination and Governance Coherence module both completed. |

Governance Coherence assessment requires competencies distinct from design adequacy assessment. The GBI assessment evaluates whether governance architecture is correctly structured. The GCI assessment evaluates whether that architecture was exercised in practice over a minimum 180-day evidence window. The evidentiary methodology, sampling approach, and professional judgement required for each are different.

Level 2 accreditation requires completion of the Governance Coherence module, covering evidence evaluation for each coherence component (Authority Adherence, Control Exercise, Drift Detection), sampling design across the 180-day assessment window, drift analysis techniques, GCI scoring mechanics, and governance agent output admissibility assessment under EIS-01 Section 7.

A Level 1 assessor may upgrade to Level 2 by completing the Governance Coherence module at any time during the accreditation period.

**Accreditation period:** 36 months from the date of grant. Renewal requires continued compliance with this Standard, completion of continuing professional development requirements, updated conflict disclosure, current professional indemnity insurance, and a satisfactory quality assurance review outcome. Renewal requirements are specified in the Assessor Qualification Framework.

## 4. Independence

Assessors must be independent of the organisation being assessed. Independence is a structural requirement, not an administrative one. A governance signal produced by the deploying organisation itself is a management representation rather than an independent assurance signal.

:::note[Normative requirement]
No assessor may certify an organisation with which it has a commercial relationship that could compromise assessment objectivity. The test is whether a reasonable institutional audience relying on the certification would consider the assessor's objectivity impaired by the relationship.
:::

### 4.1 Presumptive Independence Impairments

The following relationships constitute presumptive independence impairments. An assessor holding any of these relationships with the organisation being assessed may not conduct the assessment unless the impairment is disclosed, evaluated, and cleared through the conflict review process:

- The assessor or any member of the assessment team holds a financial interest in the organisation being assessed
- The assessor has provided advisory, consulting, or implementation services to the organisation being assessed within the preceding 24 months in relation to the governance architecture under assessment
- The assessor is engaged in active commercial negotiations with the organisation being assessed for services unrelated to the assessment
- A member of the assessment team has an employment or board relationship with the organisation being assessed, or has held such a relationship within the preceding 12 months
- The assessor has a material commercial dependency on the organisation being assessed (the organisation represents more than 25% of the assessor's revenue)
- The assessor or any member of the assessment team participated in designing, implementing, or operating the governance architecture under assessment

### 4.2 Cooling-Off Periods

| Prior Relationship | Cooling-Off Period |
|---|---|
| Advisory or consulting services on the governance architecture under assessment | 24 months from completion of advisory engagement |
| Employment or board membership at the organisation | 12 months from end of employment or board service |
| Advisory services on matters unrelated to the governance architecture under assessment | No cooling-off period, but disclosure and structural separation required |

### 4.3 Advisory and Assessment Separation

Advisory organisations may hold assessor accreditation provided advisory and assessment functions are structurally separated. Structural separation requires: different personnel on advisory and assessment engagements for the same organisation; assessment personnel do not have access to advisory engagement workpapers, commercial terms, or relationship management information; advisory and assessment revenue streams are reported separately; and the structural separation arrangement is documented and available for review by the Institute.

The separation must be in place before the assessment engagement begins. Retroactive separation does not satisfy this requirement.

### 4.4 Self-Assessment Prohibition

Self-certification does not satisfy the ARAF independence requirement. An organisation that produces its own ARAF assessment has produced a self-assessment, not a certification. Self-reported governance is evidence of intent. Independent assessment is evidence of fact.

This prohibition extends to assessments conducted by entities under common ownership or control with the organisation being assessed.

## 5. Methodology Adherence

Assessors must conduct assessments according to the current published version of the ARAF methodology. The methodology is open (licensed under CC BY 4.0), making the assessment process independently reviewable by any institutional audience that relies on the output.

Methodology adherence means that two accredited assessors assessing the same system with access to the same evidence must produce GBI composite scores within the calibration tolerance established by the Institute. Methodological consistency is the foundation of comparability. Without it, the GBI is a number rather than a signal.

Assessors must not modify the scoring framework, dimensional definitions, multiplier thresholds, or certification tier boundaries. Where interpretation questions arise, assessors must refer to the published Standard or request clarification through the standard governance process. Assessors must not introduce interpretive positions that contradict or extend the published methodology without formal guidance issued as a Governance Note.

## 6. Quality Assurance

The Institute conducts quality assurance reviews of accredited assessors. Reviews evaluate methodology adherence, scoring consistency, report quality, independence compliance, and evidence evaluation. Each accredited assessor is subject to at least one quality assurance review during each 36-month accreditation period.

Reviews may also be triggered by a complaint from an assessed organisation or institutional audience, calibration data indicating systematic scoring deviation, an adverse outcome at a certified organisation, or identification of an undisclosed conflict of interest.

| Finding | Outcome |
|---|---|
| No material findings | Accreditation continues. |
| Methodology application deficiency | Corrective action required. Supplementary training and demonstrated corrected application before further assessments. |
| Scoring inconsistency outside calibration tolerance | Recalibration required. Affected assessments flagged for institutional audience notification where the inconsistency is material to tier determination. |
| Independence violation | Immediate suspension pending investigation. Affected certifications reviewed for withdrawal. |
| Repeated or systemic quality failures | Accreditation withdrawn. Reapplication permitted after 24 months, subject to the full accreditation process. |

Where a quality assurance review identifies a deficiency material to a certification tier determination, the Institute will notify the assessed organisation and, where the deficiency cannot be remediated, notify institutional audiences that have registered reliance on the affected certification.

Assessors must cooperate with quality assurance reviews and provide documentation supporting their assessment decisions. Refusal to cooperate is grounds for accreditation suspension.

:::note[Calibration period]
During the initial deployment period (first 20 certifications), the calibration protocol defined in ARAF Standard v3.0, Clause 8.7 applies: dual-assessor review for all GCI scores, range reporting rather than point estimates, and inter-rater reliability tracking.
:::

## 7. Standard Maintainer Position

The organisation maintaining the ARAF Standard may hold assessor accreditation and conduct assessments, but only under the same independence requirements and quality assurance processes that apply to all accredited assessors. No assessment conducted by the standard maintainer carries greater or lesser authority than an assessment conducted by any other accredited assessor.

The Institute does not hold a privileged position in the assessment or certification ecosystem. Assessor accreditation, certification issuance, and quality assurance operate under defined independence requirements that apply equally to all participants, including the standard maintainer.

## 8. Assessment Reports

All ARAF Compliant and Certified assessment reports must include: Executive Summary (certification tier, GBI score, GCI scores, multiplier status, scope); Scope and Assessment Window; Decision Supply Chain map (each link, accountability assignments, contractual relationships, jurisdictional boundaries); Design Adequacy Assessment (GBI dimensional scores and findings); Governance Coherence Assessment (GCI dimensional scores and findings); Evidence Summary (evidence tier per dimension, gaps noted, governance agent output disclosure where applicable); Findings and Observations (critical, significant, informational); Remediation Roadmap (where required); and Scope Limitation and Disclaimer.

:::note[Mandatory scope limitation language]
The following language must appear verbatim in every Compliant and Certified assessment report: "This certification covers the governance architecture design and operational coherence of [Organisation] during the assessment window specified above. It does not guarantee future system performance, safety, or absence of adverse outcomes. Certification is subject to reassessment upon the occurrence of material change events as defined in the ARAF Standard Version 3.0."
:::

ARAF Assessed tier reports follow the same structure but omit the Governance Coherence Assessment section.

An assessor whose output is only a GBI score has produced a number. An assessor whose output includes a full dimensional profile, multiplier analysis, evidence quality assessment, and remediation roadmap has produced governance infrastructure.

## 9. Companion Documents

This Standard is supported by two companion documents that govern the operational detail of assessor qualification and practice.

| Document | Scope | Audience |
|---|---|---|
| Assessor Qualification Framework | Detailed competency requirements under each qualification domain, examination structure, GCI module content, calibration tolerances, and continuing professional development requirements. | Prospective and accredited assessors. |
| Assessor Operations Guide | Operational procedures for accredited assessors, including trigger documentation, methodology version transition rules, dispute resolution, data handling, and engagement management. | Accredited assessors only (restricted). |

Accredited assessors are bound by the requirements of both companion documents. The Assessor Qualification Framework will be published following completion of the pilot assessment programme. The Assessor Operations Guide is a restricted document provided to assessors upon accreditation.

## 10. Governance and Amendment

This Standard is maintained by the Institute for Autonomous Governance Pty Ltd as a supplementary specification to the ARAF Standard. Amendments follow the protocol defined in ARAF Standard v3.0, Clause 12.4: major amendments require a 60-day public consultation period and a formal version increment; minor amendments are published with a minor version increment without public consultation; interpretive guidance is issued as numbered Governance Notes.
