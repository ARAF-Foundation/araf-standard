Accreditation Requirements for ARAF Governance Assessors

ARAF certification depends on independent governance assessments conducted according to the published ARAF methodology. The credibility of certification therefore depends on the competence, independence, and accountability of the assessors who perform those assessments.

When an accredited assessor issues a certification, it accepts professional responsibility for the quality of the assessment that produced it. That accountability transfer is what gives the certification signal credibility: it is not a claim by the organisation about itself, but a representation by an independent assessor operating under a defined methodology.

The ARAF Assessor Accreditation Standard defines:

- qualification requirements for accredited assessors
- independence requirements governing assessment practice
- methodology adherence requirements
- assessment documentation requirements
- quality assurance and review procedures
- accreditation maintenance and withdrawal processes

The objective is to ensure that ARAF assessments are conducted by qualified professionals operating under consistent methodological standards and subject to professional accountability.

The accreditation architecture was designed with specific reference to the failure mode documented in the FTC's 2014 action against TRUSTe, where inadequate assessor oversight produced certifications that did not reflect actual practice. The ARAF standard addresses that risk directly. Assessors are subject to accountability review, and accreditation is contingent on maintaining assessment quality.

## 1. Role of Accredited Assessors

Accredited assessors conduct independent evaluations of autonomous system governance posture using the ARAF methodology.

An accredited assessor is authorised to:

- conduct ARAF governance assessments
- evaluate governance evidence according to the ARAF Evidence Standard
- apply the six-dimensional governance framework
- classify evidence quality tier per dimension
- calculate Governance Benchmark Index (GBI) scores
- assess Governance Coherence components and calculate Governance Coherence Index (GCI) scores for Compliant and Certified determinations
- determine multiplier conditions
- issue certification determinations where thresholds are met

The assessor's role is to evaluate governance architecture. It does not include responsibility for system design, technical development, or operational management. Assessors evaluate governance posture based on evidence produced by the organisation under assessment.

Self-assessment does not satisfy the ARAF independence requirement. An organisation that produces its own ARAF assessment has produced a self-assessment, not a certification. Self-reported governance is evidence of intent. Independent assessment is evidence of fact.

## 2. Qualification Requirements

Applicants for assessor accreditation must demonstrate competence in three domains.

### Governance Assessment Competence

Assessors must demonstrate understanding of:

- corporate governance frameworks and board accountability standards
- risk management systems and institutional risk classification
- legal accountability structures, including the standard of care applied by courts, the accountability requirements applied by regulators, and the governance expectations of insurers and investors
- organisational governance processes, including the three accountability questions: who was responsible, what did they know, what did they do

This competence ensures assessors can evaluate governance architecture rather than technical system behaviour alone. An assessor who understands model performance but not institutional accountability cannot produce an assessment that satisfies the institutional audiences the certification serves.

### Autonomous Systems Governance Literacy

Assessors must demonstrate familiarity with:

- autonomous system deployment models, including Distributed Decision Infrastructure and the Decision Supply Chain
- AI governance principles and emerging regulatory frameworks, including the EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, and applicable jurisdictional requirements
- model lifecycle management, including training, fine-tuning, retrieval-augmented generation, retraining, and versioning
- operational monitoring frameworks, including the distinction between performance monitoring and governance monitoring

Technical expertise in model development is not required, but assessors must understand the operational context in which autonomous systems operate and the governance implications of deployment decisions.

### ARAF Methodology Proficiency

Assessors must demonstrate proficiency in:

- the six-dimensional ARAF governance framework, including the sub-factors assessed within each dimension
- dimensional evidence evaluation, including evidence quality tier classification
- Decision Supply Chain mapping and evidence continuity assessment
- multiplier logic and compound weakness identification
- Governance Benchmark Index calculation and score interpretation
- certification threshold interpretation and tier determination
- assessment report production to the documentation standard specified in Section 5

This proficiency may be demonstrated through formal training, supervised assessment practice, or prior accreditation experience.

## 3. Independence Requirements

ARAF assessments must be conducted independently of the organisation being assessed. Assessors must not conduct an assessment where they have a relationship that could reasonably compromise objectivity.

Independence requirements:

- no financial interest in the organisation being assessed, including equity, debt, revenue share, or other financial exposure to the organisation's commercial performance
- no governance role within the organisation, including board membership, advisory board participation, or committee participation
- no operational responsibility for any system under assessment
- no consulting or advisory engagement directly related to the system being assessed within 12 months prior to the assessment engagement
- no involvement in the design, development, or implementation of the governance architecture being assessed

### Advisory Organisation Separation

Advisory organisations may hold assessor accreditation provided that advisory and assessment functions are structurally separated: different personnel, separate engagement terms, and documented independence protocols.

Where an organisation provides advisory services related to ARAF implementation, the individuals conducting the assessment must not have participated in those advisory engagements.

The separation must be documented and available for review during quality assurance processes. An assertion of separation without documented protocols does not satisfy the independence requirement.

### Standard Maintainer Position

The organisation maintaining the ARAF standard may hold assessor accreditation and conduct assessments, but only under the same independence requirements and quality assurance processes that apply to all accredited assessors. No assessment conducted by the standard maintainer carries greater or lesser authority than an assessment conducted by any other accredited assessor.

### Conflict Disclosure

Assessors must disclose potential conflicts of interest to the organisation being assessed and to the accreditation body before accepting any engagement. Where a disclosed conflict cannot be adequately mitigated, the assessor must decline the engagement.

## 4. Methodology Adherence

All accredited assessors must conduct assessments according to the current published version of the ARAF methodology.

This includes:

- applying the six-dimensional governance framework, including all sub-factors within each dimension
- evaluating governance evidence according to the ARAF Evidence Standard, including the four quality requirements of authenticity, integrity, traceability, and exportability
- classifying evidence quality tier per dimension (Tier 1: infrastructure-generated, Tier 2: contemporaneous documentation, Tier 3: reconstructed documentation, Tier 4: management representation)
- assessing evidence continuity across the full Decision Supply Chain
- applying multiplier logic where dimensional interactions create compound risk
- calculating Governance Benchmark Index scores using the defined methodology
- applying Governance Coherence assessment components (Authority Adherence, Control Exercise, Drift Detection) where tier determination requires coherence
- calculating GCI scores and enforcing the minimum 180-day separation between GBI assessment date and GCI assessment date for Compliant and Certified determinations
- documenting dimensional reasoning and scoring rationale for each dimension

Assessors must not modify the scoring framework, dimensional definitions, multiplier thresholds, or certification tier boundaries.

The methodology is open (licensed under CC BY 4.0), making the assessment process independently reviewable by institutional audiences that rely on the output.

Where interpretation questions arise, assessors must refer to the published standard or request clarification through the standard governance process. Assessors must not introduce interpretive positions that contradict or extend the published methodology without formal guidance from the standard governance body.

When the methodology is updated, assessors must apply the current published version in all assessments commenced after the publication date. Assessments already in progress at the time of a methodology update are completed under the version in effect when the assessment commenced.

## 5. Assessment Documentation Requirements

Each ARAF assessment must produce a structured assessment report. The report must provide sufficient explanation for institutional audiences, including boards, insurers, investors, and regulators, to understand the reasoning behind dimensional scores and the certification determination.

For ARAF Compliant and ARAF Certified outcomes, the report must explicitly separate design adequacy findings from operational coherence findings.

The assessment report must include the following required sections:

- Executive Summary
- Scope and Assessment Window
- Design Adequacy Assessment (GBI)
- Governance Coherence Assessment (GCI)
- Evidence Summary
- Findings and Observations
- Scope Limitation and Disclaimer

The report must also include:

- System description: description of the system under assessment, including the autonomous system, the decisions it is authorised to make, and the operational environment
- Decision Supply Chain map: identification of each link in the chain, accountability assignments, contractual relationships, and jurisdictional boundaries
- Dimensional profile: individual scores for all six dimensions, with scoring rationale for each dimension
- Evidence quality assessment: evidence quality tier classification per dimension, with explanation of the basis for each classification
- GBI composite score: calculated according to the published methodology
- GCI dimensional profile and combined GCI where coherence is required for tier determination
- Multiplier analysis: identification of any multiplier conditions present, with explanation of the compound governance exposure each condition produces
- Evidence summary: description of the governance evidence reviewed, organised by evidence category (design, deployment, operational, and outcome evidence), with identification of evidence gaps
- Remediation roadmap: where governance weaknesses are identified, specific remediation recommendations distinguishing structural risks from operational risks, with implications for remediation timelines and costs
- Certification determination: certification tier where thresholds are satisfied, or explanation of why certification thresholds were not met
- Assessment metadata: assessment date, validity period, assessor identity, accreditation reference, and methodology version applied

Required scope limitation language:

"This certification covers the governance architecture design and operational coherence of [Organisation] during the assessment window specified above. It does not guarantee future system performance, safety, or absence of adverse outcomes. Certification is subject to reassessment upon the occurrence of material change events as defined in the ARAF standard."

An assessor whose output is only a GBI score has produced a number. An assessor whose output includes a full dimensional profile, multiplier analysis, evidence quality assessment, and remediation roadmap has produced governance infrastructure.

## 6. Reassessment Trigger Obligations

Assessors must apply mandatory reassessment triggers consistently across certified organisations. The following events invalidate coherence assessment and require reassessment before certified status can be maintained:

- material change to system autonomy level (increase of more than one Autonomy Gradient level)
- substitution of a primary AI provider or model
- significant expansion of operational scope beyond assessed scope
- a material incident where governance architecture was engaged, tested, or found insufficient
- discovery of systematic governance drift outside the normal assessment cycle

Certified organisations must notify their accredited assessor of triggering events within 60 days of occurrence.

Assessors must document:

- trigger event date and notification date
- determination on whether reassessment is mandatory
- date reassessment was initiated

Continued use of certification after a triggering event without initiating reassessment constitutes a certification breach and must be recorded in assessor documentation.

## 7. Quality Assurance and Review

The ARAF certification ecosystem includes quality assurance mechanisms to maintain assessment consistency and credibility.

Quality assurance processes include:

- periodic review of assessment reports for methodological consistency and documentation completeness
- peer review of dimensional scoring decisions across comparable governance postures
- evaluation of evidence interpretation practices, including evidence quality tier classification consistency
- investigation of certification disputes raised by assessed organisations or institutional stakeholders
- review of assessor independence documentation

Assessors must provide documentation supporting their assessment decisions during quality assurance reviews. Refusal to cooperate with quality assurance processes is grounds for accreditation suspension.

Quality assurance findings are used to identify systemic issues across the assessor ecosystem and to inform methodology clarifications issued through the standard governance process.

## 8. Assessment Dispute Resolution

Where an organisation disagrees with an assessment outcome, the following dispute resolution process applies.

### Assessor Review

The organisation raises the specific dimensional scores or findings in dispute, with supporting evidence. The assessor reviews the disputed items and provides a written response explaining the scoring rationale or, where warranted, revising the assessment.

### Independent Review

If the dispute is not resolved at the assessor level, the organisation may request independent review by a second accredited assessor not involved in the original assessment. The independent reviewer evaluates the disputed dimensions against the published methodology and the evidence record.

### Standard Governance Referral

Disputes involving interpretation of the published methodology, rather than application of the methodology to evidence, may be referred to the standard governance body for interpretive guidance through a published governance note.

The dispute resolution process does not permit modification of the scoring framework or certification thresholds. It addresses whether the methodology was correctly applied to the evidence presented.

## 9. Accreditation Maintenance

Assessor accreditation must be maintained through continued compliance with the accreditation standard.

Accredited assessors must:

- maintain familiarity with updates to the ARAF methodology and apply the current published version in all assessments
- conduct assessments according to the documentation standard specified in Section 5
- cooperate with quality assurance review processes
- disclose potential conflicts of interest before accepting engagements
- maintain professional competence in governance assessment, autonomous systems governance, and ARAF methodology as the standard evolves
- complete any continuing professional development requirements established by the accreditation body

Failure to maintain these requirements results in accreditation suspension or withdrawal.

## 10. Accreditation Withdrawal

Accreditation is withdrawn where an assessor fails to meet the requirements of the accreditation standard.

Grounds for withdrawal include:

- persistent failure to apply the ARAF methodology correctly
- failure to maintain independence from assessed organisations
- undisclosed conflicts of interest
- refusal to cooperate with quality assurance reviews
- repeated issuance of certifications inconsistent with the evidence reviewed
- failure to produce assessment documentation meeting the standard specified in Section 5
- sustained quality failures identified through the quality assurance process

Where accreditation is withdrawn, the assessor may no longer conduct ARAF assessments or issue certification determinations. Certifications previously issued by a withdrawn assessor are not automatically invalidated, but organisations holding those certifications may be subject to reassessment by a different accredited assessor.

## 11. Relationship to Standard Governance

The ARAF standard defines the assessment methodology. The Assessor Accreditation Standard defines who may apply that methodology in certification assessments.

This separation follows established precedent in standards ecosystems:

- ISO develops standards; accredited certification bodies conduct audits
- PCI Security Standards Council maintains PCI DSS; Qualified Security Assessors conduct compliance assessments
- CREST maintains penetration testing standards; accredited firms conduct assessments against that standard

ARAF follows the same structural separation.

The standard maintainer does not hold a privileged position within the assessment ecosystem. All accredited assessors operate under the same methodology, independence requirements, and quality assurance processes.

When standard governance produces a material methodology change, that change flows through to the assessment ecosystem. Assessors are responsible for applying the current published version of the methodology in all assessments. Certified organisations are subject to the reassessment obligations described in the Certification Lifecycle.

## 12. The Assessor Ecosystem

The ARAF certification ecosystem depends on independent assessors drawn from disciplines with relevant governance assessment expertise. As adoption expands, the assessor ecosystem is expected to include:

- independent governance consultancies
- specialist risk advisory firms
- law firms with governance assessment expertise
- audit and assurance organisations
- technical governance specialists with institutional risk experience
- insurance advisory firms with AI governance capability

The accreditation standard applies uniformly regardless of the assessor's professional background or organisational type. The qualifying requirement is demonstrated competence in the three domains specified in Section 2, not membership of a particular professional category.

Additional training and accreditation pathways will be introduced as the ecosystem develops. The objective is to maintain consistent assessment quality while enabling a diverse, independent assessor ecosystem.
