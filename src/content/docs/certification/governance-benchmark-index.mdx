The Governance Benchmark Index (GBI) is the normative scoring methodology for ARAF assessments. It translates six-dimensional governance assessment into a comparable institutional signal. This page specifies how the score is calculated. For how institutional audiences interpret and rely upon GBI scores and ARAF certification, see the [Certification Framework](/certification/certification-framework/).

---

> The comparability of the GBI depends on the stability of the underlying standard.
> The ARAF standard is maintained under a Founding Stewardship governance model to ensure consistency of scoring logic, multiplier application, and certification thresholds across assessment periods.

---

## 1. Purpose of the GBI

Institutional audiences need comparability. Boards, insurers, investors, and regulators cannot each re-run a full governance assessment every time they encounter an autonomous system deployment. They need a way to understand governance posture in a form that is comparable across entities, understandable without reproducing the full methodology, and grounded in a structured assessment framework.

The GBI performs that function. It is the governance equivalent of a credit rating in bond markets or a classification society survey in marine insurance: an independently produced, comparably expressed signal that allows risk to be assessed rather than excluded.

The underlying principle the GBI serves is that institutions can rely on autonomous systems only to the extent that the decisions those systems produce can be reconstructed from contemporaneous governance records. The GBI score is the compressed signal that communicates whether that condition is met.

GBI scores should be read as indicators of governance risk intensity rather than as abstract quality scores. The scale runs from 1.0 (strongest governance posture) to 5.0 (critical governance exposure). This inversion is intentional. It mirrors the logic of credit ratings and classification society surveys: lower scores indicate greater institutional reliability.

Because autonomous systems do not fail through isolated weaknesses alone, the GBI does not treat dimensional scores as simply additive. Where compound weaknesses exist across dimensions, multiplier logic applies to reflect the amplified systemic exposure.

---

## 2. Scoring Logic

Each autonomous system deployment is assessed across six dimensions:

- Autonomy Gradient
- Data Sensitivity Exposure
- Contract Infrastructure
- Liability Architecture
- Commercial Leverage
- Adaptive Stability

The six ARAF dimensions are governance architecture dimensions, not AI ethics principles. Each one assesses a structural property of the Decision Supply Chain that determines whether autonomous outcomes can be traced, attributed, and governed within institutional accountability structures. The GBI aggregates these assessments into a single comparable governance signal.

Each dimension receives a score from 1.0 to 5.0. The scale is intentionally inverted relative to intuition. It mirrors the logic of credit ratings: 1.0 represents the strongest governance posture and 5.0 represents critical governance exposure. The choice of a numeric scale rather than a letter-grade system preserves the granularity that institutional audiences, particularly insurers and investors, require for pricing and diligence.

### Scale Interpretation

| Score Range | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| 1.0&ndash;1.75 | Strong governance posture |
| 1.76&ndash;2.50 | Minimum institutional threshold met |
| 2.51&ndash;3.50 | Material governance weakness requiring remediation |
| 3.51&ndash;5.00 | High governance risk / institutional inadequacy |

Higher scores indicate greater governance risk, not higher governance maturity.

## 3. Composite Score

The GBI composite score is derived from the six-dimensional assessment and adjusted by multiplier logic where compound weaknesses are present.

The composite score answers a narrow but important question: what is the overall governance posture of this deployment in institutionally comparable form? The dimensional profile answers the broader governance question: where, specifically, does the exposure sit? Both outputs are required. The GBI produces comparability. The dimensional profile produces actionability.

### Composite Score Calculation

The GBI composite score is calculated in two steps.

**Step 1: Base composite.** Sum the six dimensional scores and divide by six. Each dimension contributes equally to the base composite.

**Step 2: Multiplier adjustment.** Identify active multipliers against the trigger conditions defined in Section 4. Add the value of each active multiplier to the base composite. Where two or more multipliers are active simultaneously, all values are added (additive, not sequential). The composite score is capped at 5.0.

### Worked Example: Single Multiplier

**Dimensional scores:** D1 = 3.0, D2 = 2.0, D3 = 4.0, D4 = 2.5, D5 = 1.5, D6 = 2.0

**Base composite:** (3.0 + 2.0 + 4.0 + 2.5 + 1.5 + 2.0) / 6 = 15.0 / 6 = 2.50

**Active multiplier:** Infrastructure Collapse (+2), triggered by D3 &ge; 4.0 and D1 &ge; 3.0

**GBI = 2.50 + 2 = 4.50**

Certification outcome: above 2.50 threshold. Remediation required.

### Worked Example: Dual Multiplier

**Dimensional scores:** D1 = 4.0, D2 = 1.5, D3 = 4.0, D4 = 4.0, D5 = 2.0, D6 = 2.0

**Base composite:** (4.0 + 1.5 + 4.0 + 4.0 + 2.0 + 2.0) / 6 = 17.5 / 6 = 2.92

**Active multipliers:**
- Systemic Escalation (+3): triggered by D1 &ge; 4.0 and D4 &ge; 4.0
- Infrastructure Collapse (+2): triggered by D3 &ge; 4.0 and D1 &ge; 3.0

**GBI = 2.92 + 3 + 2 = 7.92, capped at 5.0**

Certification outcome: 5.0. Structural failure. Both multiplier conditions are active. Remediation must address D4 (liability architecture) and D3 (contract infrastructure) before any certification tier is achievable. Remediating D4 below 4.0 deactivates Systemic Escalation and removes +3 from the composite. Remediating D3 below 4.0 deactivates Infrastructure Collapse and removes +2. The remediation roadmap should sequence D4 first: it participates in the higher-value multiplier.

This example demonstrates that dual multiplier activation produces a cap condition. The base composite of 2.92 would otherwise qualify for the remediation range. The compound weaknesses across D1, D3, and D4 produce a governance condition that the base composite alone does not reflect.

### Normative Status

The composite score calculation methodology described in this section is the canonical scoring methodology referenced in ARAF Standard v3.0, Clause 7.2. Any assessor applying this methodology to identical dimensional inputs must produce an identical GBI composite score. The weighting methodology is subject to calibration review under ARAF Standard v3.0, Clause 8.7.

## 4. Multiplier Logic

The six dimensions are not independent. Weaknesses in one dimension compound weaknesses in others. ARAF incorporates multiplier logic to capture this compounding effect.

### Primary Multiplier Combinations

| Multiplier | Trigger | Penalty | Governance Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Systemic Escalation | D1 &ge; 4.0 and D4 &ge; 4.0 | +3 | High autonomy combined with inadequate liability architecture. The volume of autonomous action consequences is high and the liability governance is inadequate to manage them. |
| Infrastructure Collapse | D3 &ge; 4.0 and D1 &ge; 3.0 | +2 | Significant autonomy combined with inadequate contract infrastructure. Liability allocation at the execution boundary is ungoverned contractually. |
| Leverage Collapse | D5 &ge; 4.0 and D4 &ge; 3.0 | +2 | High commercial dependency combined with inadequate liability architecture. The system is structurally resistant to remediation. |

Individual dimensional weaknesses are often manageable. Compound weaknesses create systemic exposure because compounding reduces the options available for remediation and increases the cost of each option. Where a multiplier condition is present, the composite score must not be read as a simple average. It reflects compounded governance exposure.

### Compound Activation Principle

The compound risk of the dimensional combination, not the mechanical threshold alone, determines multiplier activation. Where a dimensional combination creates systemic exposure and one score falls marginally below a stated threshold, the assessor must evaluate whether the combination produces the governance condition the multiplier is designed to capture.

Mechanical thresholds are the default. They are not a ceiling on assessor judgement. An assessment that applies thresholds mechanically without evaluating the governance substance those thresholds are designed to detect has not satisfied the assessment standard.

### Infrastructure Collapse: Expanded Assessment

Infrastructure Collapse applies where autonomous decision systems depend on infrastructure whose availability, behaviour, or governance cannot be reliably controlled by the deploying organisation. This includes dependencies created through contractual arrangements with upstream AI providers whose termination rights, policy authority, or unilateral modification powers could materially disrupt system operation or remove governance authority from the deploying organisation.

**Trigger conditions.** Infrastructure Collapse (+2) is triggered when one or more of the following conditions are present:

- **Upstream governance dependency.** Critical AI infrastructure is controlled by an external provider whose contractual rights exceed the governance authority of the deploying organisation.
- **Termination dependency.** A provider can suspend or terminate access to infrastructure required for system operation.
- **Policy governance dependency.** Upstream acceptable use policies allow the provider to veto or suspend deployment categories operated by the deploying organisation.
- **Unilateral model modification risk.** The provider may change model behaviour, training, or safety parameters without the deploying organisation's governance approval or prior notice.
- **Single-provider infrastructure concentration.** No operational fallback capability exists if the primary provider becomes unavailable.

### Dimension 3 Additive Assessment Items

**Item A &mdash; Upstream Governance Dependency**

*Assessment question:* Do upstream AI provider agreements allocate operational liability to the deploying organisation while withholding governance authority, including audit rights, model change notification, or behavioural guarantees?

Failure indicators: provider liability exclusions for AI outputs; no audit rights over model behaviour; unilateral model modification rights; no notification obligations for material model updates.

**Item B &mdash; Policy Governance Dependency**

*Assessment question:* Do upstream provider acceptable use policies impose governance constraints on the deploying organisation's autonomous system operations that the organisation cannot contractually resist or anticipate?

Failure indicators: provider discretion to suspend deployment categories; unclear policy interpretation mechanisms; no contractual protection against retrospective policy change.

### Dimension 6 Additive Assessment Item

**Infrastructure Dependency Stability**

*Assessment question:* Does the organisation rely on upstream AI providers whose service termination or unilateral modification could materially disrupt operational decision systems?

*Evidence required:* provider contracts; dependency mapping documentation; fallback architecture documentation; operational contingency plan.

Failure indicators: no alternative provider capability; no internal fallback system; termination clauses permitting immediate suspension; no documented operational contingency.

*Resilience diagnostic:* Can the system continue operating if the primary model provider becomes unavailable for 72 hours? If the answer is no, the Dimension 6 score increases.

## 5. GBI and GCI Role Split

The GBI and GCI serve different functions and must be interpreted together for Compliant and Certified determinations.

The **GBI** evaluates design adequacy: whether governance architecture is correctly structured for the system's risk profile. The **GCI** evaluates operational coherence: whether that architecture was actually exercised over a defined assessment window.

In practical terms, GBI answers whether governance was designed correctly. GCI answers whether governance operated correctly. Compliant and Certified determinations require both conditions.

The GCI is scored on a 0.0 to 1.0 scale per dimension. The GCI functions as a modifier on the base GBI dimensional scores through the penalty formula:

> **Effective Dimension Score = GBI Dimension Score &times; (2 &minus; GCI Dimension Score)**

This formula penalises low GCI scores by increasing the effective dimension score. An organisation with strong governance design (low GBI score) but poor operational coherence (GCI below 1.0) will have its effective certification posture degraded to reflect operational reality.

:::note[Reference]
The coherence assessment methodology is specified in ARAF Standard v3.0, Clause 8. The GCI methodology, previously published as the Governance Coherence Addendum (GCA-01), was absorbed into the Standard effective March 2026.
:::

## 6. Evidence Quality Tiers

The GBI score communicates governance posture. The evidence quality tier communicates the confidence warranted in that assessment.

Not all governance evidence supporting a GBI score is equal. ARAF distinguishes four evidence quality tiers. Tier 4 (management representation) is not admissible for coherence determination.

| Tier | Evidence Type | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 1: Infrastructure-generated | Contemporaneous, tamper-evident records produced by governance infrastructure as a natural output of governance operations | Highest. Produced at the moment of governance exercise, in a form the organisation cannot alter retroactively. |
| Tier 2: Contemporaneous documentation | Records produced at the time of governance decisions (board papers, AIOC minutes, deployment approval records) | High for documented decisions. Depends on consistency and completeness of documentation practices. |
| Tier 3: Reconstructed documentation | Records assembled in response to assessment requests, reconstructing governance claims from existing materials | Lower. Produced after the fact, subject to selection and framing choices. May limit certification tier achievable. |
| Tier 4: Management representation | Formal written representation where no contemporaneous record exists | Not admissible for coherence determination. If primary for a control, the control is assessed as not evidenced. |

Two identical GBI scores may carry different institutional confidence depending on evidence tier. The standard for ARAF evidence is not what the organisation says about its governance. It is what the governance infrastructure produces.

EIS-01 defines the minimum structure of a compliant Tier 1 evidence record: four required components (Execution Event, Contextual State, Authority and Attribution, and Integrity Anchor). Assessors evaluate whether evidence satisfies these component requirements as part of every Compliant and Certified assessment.

## 7. Certification Thresholds

The GBI anchors the certification thresholds within ARAF. The thresholds are governance thresholds, not actuarial thresholds. They are derived from the bankability conditions (identifiability, measurability, governability, insurability) that the framework establishes.

| Certification Tier | Threshold | Institutional Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ARAF Assessed | Assessment completed; no minimum score required | Governance posture has been independently evaluated and is available for institutional review. Entry level. Minimum for institutional discussions. |
| ARAF Compliant | GBI &le; 2.50 with GCI &ge; 0.70 | Minimum institutional threshold. Design adequacy and operational coherence both demonstrated for institutional reliance in most deployment contexts. |
| ARAF Certified | GBI &le; 1.75 with GCI &ge; 0.85 | Full agentic bankability conditions met. Strong design adequacy and strong operational coherence support classification, insurance, financing, and institutional reliance. |

These thresholds do not merely label governance posture. They define whether the governance architecture has reached a level that supports institutional reliance. For how institutional audiences apply these thresholds in practice, see the [Certification Framework](/certification/certification-framework/).

---

## 8. Assessment Report Requirements

Every GBI output must be accompanied by: individual dimensional scores; multiplier analysis; evidence quality tier per dimension; and a remediation roadmap where required.

An assessor whose output is only a number has produced a score. An assessor whose output includes a full dimensional profile, multiplier analysis, evidence quality assessment, and remediation roadmap has produced governance infrastructure.

The complete assessment report structure is specified in ARAF Standard v3.0, Clause 11.4.
