ARAF assesses governance posture across six dimensions. Each dimension captures a distinct category of governance risk. Together they produce a composite picture of the governance architecture of an autonomous system deployment.

The six dimensions are designed to answer the three accountability questions that institutional governance requires: who was responsible, what did they know, what did they do. Each dimension produces the governance information needed to answer those questions for a different category of risk.

A classification without a dimensional profile has produced a score. A classification with a dimensional profile has produced governance infrastructure: The governing principle is that institutions can rely on autonomous systems only to the extent that the decisions those systems produce can be reconstructed from contemporaneous governance records. The six dimensions define what must be governed. The evidence standard defines what must be recorded. Together they determine whether the reconstruction the three accountability questions require is possible. Governance infrastructure provides the information that allows remediation to be targeted, governance investment to be prioritised, and institutional audiences to understand where governance strength and weakness sit.

## Governance Architecture, Not Governance Principles

The ARAF dimensions are structural assessment dimensions. Each one corresponds to a property of the Decision Supply Chain through which autonomous decisions are produced:

- D1 - Autonomy Gradient: the degree of independent decision authority the system exercises
- D2 - Data Sensitivity Exposure: the sensitivity classification of data the system ingests, processes, or acts upon
- D3 - Contract Infrastructure: whether contractual arrangements govern responsibility across each party in the Decision Supply Chain
- D4 - Liability Architecture: whether liability for autonomous outcomes is allocated, insurable, and enforceable
- D5 - Commercial Leverage: the deploying organisation's ability to enforce governance requirements against its vendors
- D6 - Adaptive Stability: whether governance controls remain effective as the system's behaviour changes over time

These dimensions allow governance posture to be measured, compared, and certified through the Governance Benchmark Index.

Many AI governance frameworks organise guidance around principles: transparency, fairness, accountability, safety, privacy. Those principles describe governance objectives. ARAF dimensions measure whether the governance architecture required to achieve those objectives exists.

## Overview

D1: Autonomy Gradient. Operational autonomy level, commitment authority, exception handling, scope boundaries, and oversight adequacy.

D2: Data Sensitivity Exposure. Operational data sensitivity and training data provenance, two distinct risk categories, both assessed.

D3: Contract Infrastructure. Customer agreements, vendor agreements, data processing agreements, and liability provisions.

D4: Liability Architecture. Autonomous action consequences, liability caps, carve-out structure, and insurance coverage for autonomous action consequences.

D5: Commercial Leverage. Operational dependency, revenue concentration, remediation commercial resistance, and lock-in dynamics.

D6: Adaptive Stability. Governance maintenance capacity as the system evolves: update governance, change triggers, monitoring architecture.

## Dimension 1: Autonomy Gradient

The Autonomy Gradient is the foundational dimension. It establishes the governance burden that all other dimensions must address. A system operating at low autonomy with weak contract infrastructure faces a different risk profile than a system operating at high autonomy with the same contract weakness.

### What it assesses

Dimension 1 assesses five sub-factors:

Operational autonomy level. What decisions does the system make without requiring human authorisation for each individual decision? What categories of action does the system take autonomously?

Commitment authority. What is the maximum financial or operational commitment the system can make autonomously? Commitments below a defined threshold may be appropriate for autonomous execution; commitments above that threshold require governance attention proportional to their scale.

Exception handling. What happens when the system encounters a decision that falls outside its trained parameters? The exception handling design reveals whether autonomy boundaries are enforced in practice or only in specification.

Scope boundary enforcement. How are the system's operational scope boundaries enforced technically and contractually? Scope drift, where a system begins operating beyond its defined parameters, is a governance failure that Dimension 1 is designed to detect.

Human oversight adequacy. Is the human oversight structure adequate for the autonomy level at which the system operates? A supervisory governance structure applied to a high-autonomy deployment is not an oversight structure. It is the appearance of oversight without the substance.

### Institutional implication

Boards exercising governance over a high-autonomy system have materially different obligations than boards exercising governance over a supervised system. Insurers underwriting coverage for a high-autonomy deployment face materially different risk than insurers covering a human-supervised process. The Autonomy Gradient establishes those differences in quantified form.

Dimension 1 maps to the inference boundary in the governance architecture: the point at which model interaction occurs, where jurisdictional accountability for AI inference is established, and where governance telemetry must record what the system processed and under whose authority.

> A governance structure designed for supervised systems applied to autonomous ones is not a defence. It is an exhibit.

## Dimension 2: Data Sensitivity Exposure

Data Sensitivity Exposure assesses two distinct but related risk categories. Operational data sensitivity creates immediate exposure. Training data provenance creates latent exposure: risks embedded at the time of training that surface through litigation, regulatory inquiry, and investor diligence, often years later.

### Operational data sensitivity

The system is assessed against the categories of data it processes in production:

●	personal information

●	health information

●	financial information

●	commercially sensitive information

●	data subject to jurisdictional restrictions on cross-border transfer

### Training data provenance

Training data provenance is assessed as a separate risk category. The relevant questions are:

Foundation model layer. What representations has the foundation model developer made about training data provenance? What warranties do those representations carry? What indemnification is available if provenance issues surface?

Fine-tuning layer. What is the legal basis for using each category of data in fine-tuning? What intellectual property rights does the organisation hold? What consent did the individuals whose data was used provide? What documentation exists to support those positions?

Retrieval-augmented generation layer. Where the system retrieves content from external sources at inference time, what copyright exposure does that retrieval create? What accuracy exposure arises where retrieved content is incorrect? What data protection exposure arises where it includes personal information?

### The backward-compounding characteristic

Training data provenance risk is backward-compounding: the exposure is created at the moment of training but compounds across the system's entire operational history. Every decision the system makes from deployment forward is a decision made by a system trained on that data.

Dimension 2 maps to the data boundary in the governance architecture: the point at which governance determines whether the system's inputs are legally and regulatorily defensible before any model invocation occurs.

Contemporaneous provenance documentation is the mechanism that answers the liability question with evidence rather than reconstruction. The provenance requirement applies bilaterally. An insurer using AI to process submission data in its own underwriting pipeline faces the same data provenance governance requirement as the organisation whose system it is underwriting. Where underwriting decisions rely on AI-transformed data that cannot be traced back to the original submission, the formation record fragments, and formation defects are where ab initio challenges gain traction. It cannot be produced retrospectively. It must be established at the time of training or fine-tuning.

## Dimension 3: Contract Infrastructure

Contract infrastructure is the governance layer closest to commercial consequence. Gaps in contract infrastructure become visible at the worst possible time: when an adverse outcome has occurred and the parties are determining who bears the loss.

### What it assesses

Master services agreement maturity. Do customer agreements contain AI-specific provisions?

Vendor agreement adequacy. Do agreements with AI infrastructure providers, foundation model developers, and data suppliers address the governance obligations those relationships create?

Data processing agreements. Are data processing agreements in place and adequate for the categories of data the system processes, including training data where applicable?

Liability adequacy. Do liability provisions reflect the actual exposure created by the system's autonomous operation, or do they reflect the exposure of a conventional software service?

Negotiation governance. Does the organisation have a defined process for reviewing and approving deviations from standard contract positions?

Political force majeure provisions. Do commercial agreements address government supply chain risk designation events as a distinct trigger, separate from technical failure or standard force majeure? Standard SaaS force majeure clauses do not cover provider designation events. Agreements executed after March 2026 that do not include political force majeure provisions for deployments relying on a single US-hosted provider carry an unaddressed structural gap.

Provider substitution rights with political designation trigger. Are substitution rights in customer and vendor agreements limited to technical API failure, or do they explicitly extend to government supply chain risk designation events affecting the primary AI provider?

### The timing problem

Contract infrastructure gaps are invisible until they matter. An organisation operating under an agreement that lacks AI-specific liability provisions may operate without incident for years. When an adverse outcome occurs, the absence of adequate contract infrastructure becomes the central issue in determining who bears the loss.

Dimension 3 interacts with the action boundary in the governance architecture: contract infrastructure is the legal expression of the rules governing what the system is permitted to do. Where the governance enforcement layer operationalises those rules at runtime, contract infrastructure establishes their legal authority.

The February 2026 Anthropic-Pentagon designation event demonstrated that a government supply chain risk designation operates in hours, requires no judicial review, and cascades through enterprise customer supply chains contractually rather than technically. Enterprises whose AI provider is designated do not lose API access. Their customers become non-compliant if they continue using the product. This cascade mechanism is a D3 assessment item because the gap is contractual: no existing standard SaaS contract template addresses it. See [ARAF Assessor Guidance Note GN-002: Cross-Border Deployment Assessment](/assessor-guidance/gn-002/) for detailed cross-border liability architecture assessment.

## Dimension 4: Liability Architecture

Liability Architecture is the dimension that most directly addresses the accountability question that autonomous systems create. It assesses how liability for the system's autonomous decisions is structured, documented, and governed.

### Autonomous Action Consequences

The central concept in Dimension 4 is the category of autonomous action consequences: the outcomes produced by the system's decisions without per-step human authorisation.

This is the category of liability that existing frameworks were not designed to address:

●	professional indemnity frameworks were designed for the negligence of a professional person, not for the autonomous decisions of a software system

●	product liability frameworks were designed for defective goods, not for the consequences of software-generated decisions at machine scale

●	technology errors and omissions frameworks were designed for system failures, not for decisions the system made correctly according to its design but that produced adverse outcomes

### What it assesses

Recognition. Has the organisation explicitly identified and documented the autonomous action consequences category for each material autonomous system?

Liability cap adequacy. Are liability caps in commercial agreements adequate for the actual exposure created by the system's autonomous operation?

Carve-out structure. Do carve-outs in liability provisions leave material exposure ungoverned?

Insurance coverage. Does the organisation's insurance coverage address the consequences of autonomous system decisions?

### Institutional implication

Dimension 4 maps directly to the underwriting intake requirement: who had authority to commit the organisation to the decision that created the exposure, and what evidence existed at the time that authority was exercised? An insurer assessing AE3 coverage needs the Dimension 4 profile to determine whether the liability architecture is adequate for the autonomy level at which the system operates. Without Dimension 4 governance, the autonomous action consequences exposure is unbounded, and unbounded exposure cannot be underwritten.

## Dimension 5: Commercial Leverage

Commercial Leverage assesses the degree to which the organisation has become operationally dependent on the system in ways that create governance vulnerability.

### The leverage dynamic

The leverage dynamic emerges when a system becomes embedded in the organisation's commercial relationships in ways that make remediation commercially difficult. The commercial disruption required to remediate governance creates pressure to defer remediation. Governance gaps that would otherwise be addressed remain open because the cost of closing them has become commercially visible.

### What it assesses

●	revenue concentration

●	customer relationship embedding

●	remediation commercial resistance

●	technology lock-in

●	Government-adjacent customer exposure: whether the customer base includes entities holding government, defence, or regulated procurement relationships that create cascade risk in the event of a provider designation. This is the mechanism through which a government action against the AI provider converts customer concentration into existential revenue loss for the deploying organisation. The assessment identifies which customers hold government procurement obligations and what proportion of revenue they represent.

Commercial leverage is the dimension that most directly explains why governance gaps, once opened, tend to remain open.

### Institutional implication

For investors, Dimension 5 is a structural risk signal. A high D5 score indicates that governance remediation, even if technically straightforward, will face commercial resistance. The remediation timeline and cost are not determined by the governance deficit alone; they are determined by the commercial disruption remediation requires. An investor who sees a high D5 score alongside a high D3 or D4 score is looking at a governance deficit that the organisation's commercial structure is working against correcting.

## Dimension 6: Adaptive Stability

Adaptive Stability assesses the organisation's capacity to maintain adequate governance as the system evolves. An organisation that has adequate governance at the point of initial deployment may have inadequate governance six months later if its governance architecture does not evolve with the system.

### What it assesses

●	governance maintenance processes

●	reassessment triggers

●	change governance

●	monitoring architecture

●	provider designation monitoring: whether the organisation monitors for government actions, including supply chain risk designations, affecting its primary AI provider stack. A government designation event is a reassessment trigger under D6 because it can invalidate the governance architecture within hours.

A system that generates extensive performance metrics but no governance records has monitoring without accountability.

### Institutional implication

For insurers, Dimension 6 is the ongoing governance maintenance signal across the coverage period. An ARAF assessment captures governance posture at a point in time. Dimension 6 assesses whether the organisation has the infrastructure to maintain that posture between assessments. A low D6 score at the time of assessment is a positive signal. A high D6 score means the governance posture the insurer priced at policy inception may not be the governance posture operating at the time of a claim.

## The Multiplier Logic

The six dimensions are not independent. Weaknesses in one dimension compound weaknesses in others. ARAF incorporates multiplier logic that captures this compounding effect.

### Primary Multiplier Combinations

| Multiplier | Trigger | Compounding exposure |
|---|---|---|
| Systemic Escalation | D1 >= 4 AND D4 >= 4 | High autonomy combined with inadequate liability architecture. The most severe compound exposure. |
| Infrastructure Collapse | D3 >= 4 AND D1 >= 3 | Significant autonomy combined with inadequate contract infrastructure. Liability allocation at the execution boundary is ungoverned contractually. |
| Leverage Collapse | D5 >= 4 AND D4 >= 3 | High commercial dependency combined with inadequate liability architecture. The system is structurally resistant to remediation. |
| Political Cascade | D5 >= 4 AND D3 >= 3 with single-provider dependency and government-adjacent customer concentration | High commercial dependency combined with inadequate political force majeure contract provisions. Provider designation converts customer concentration into revenue loss. |

### What the multiplier logic means in practice

Individual dimensional weaknesses are manageable. Compound weaknesses create systemic exposure because the compounding reduces the options available for remediation and increases the cost of each option.
