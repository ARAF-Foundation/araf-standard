## Overview

Six dimensions. One composite score.

The Six ARAF Dimensions provide the governance assessment framework for autonomous system deployments. They define how ARAF classifies, measures, and benchmarks governance posture across six dimensions that determine institutional readiness.

- Published by: Institute for Autonomous Governance Pty Ltd
- Date: March 2026
- Version: 3.0
- Reference: araf-standard.org

The six dimensions are:

- D1 - Autonomy Gradient: what decisions the system makes without human authorisation for each individual decision.
- D2 - Data Sensitivity Exposure: what data the system processes and where that data comes from.
- D3 - Contract Infrastructure: what legal architecture governs the deployment.
- D4 - Liability Architecture: how liability for autonomous decisions is structured, documented, and governed.
- D5 - Commercial Leverage: how commercially dependent the organisation is on the system.
- D6 - Adaptive Stability: whether governance can be maintained as the system and deployment evolve.

### ARAF Six-Dimensional Governance Framework

![Six governance dimensions — multiplier interaction topology](../../../public/images/araf/araf_six_dimensions_interaction_hexagon.png)

Governance Benchmark Index scores range from 1.0 to 5.0. Lower scores indicate stronger governance posture.

Four structural multipliers - Systemic Escalation, Infrastructure Collapse, Leverage Collapse, and Political Cascade - can compound scores where dimensional combinations exceed activation thresholds.

The six dimensions are assessed independently but interpreted together through the Governance Benchmark Index (GBI) composite score and multiplier logic.

## 1. Autonomy Gradient (D1)

What decisions does the system make without requiring human authorisation for each individual decision?

The Autonomy Gradient is the foundational dimension. Its score determines the governance burden all other dimensions must address. A system operating at high autonomy with weak liability architecture or contract infrastructure creates compounding exposure that multiplier logic is designed to capture.

### What it assesses

- Operational autonomy level and categories of action performed without per-decision human authorisation.
- Commitment authority, including maximum financial or operational commitment executed autonomously.
- Exception handling for decisions outside trained parameters.
- Scope boundary enforcement through technical and contractual controls.
- Human oversight adequacy proportionate to deployed autonomy level.

### Institutional implication

D1 is foundational for boards, insurers, investors, and regulators because it determines exposure scale at the point of autonomous decision execution.

### Evidence profile

Machine-enforceable evidence is available for part of D1. Deterministic enforcement logs, including `FAIL_CLOSED`, `HARD_BLOCK` on scope violations, and `PASS_CONFIRMED` on benign inputs, can provide Tier 1 D1 evidence. Human oversight structure still requires institutional-layer evidence.

## 2. Data Sensitivity Exposure (D2)

What data does the system process, and where does that data come from?

Data Sensitivity Exposure assesses two risk categories that require separate governance treatment. Operational data creates immediate exposure at the point of processing. Training data provenance creates latent exposure carried into every downstream decision.

### What it assesses

- Operational data categories, including sensitivity classification, access governance, and handling controls.
- Training data provenance, including legal basis, IP clearance, and consent frameworks for training and fine-tuning datasets.
- Foundation model provider representations on training data governance.
- Retraining cycles as new provenance governance triggers.
- RAG provenance exposure where inference-time retrieval is deployed.

### Institutional implication

For boards and legal counsel, D2 maps latent liability created before deployment. Provenance gaps identified post-deployment cannot be remediated retroactively because they are embedded in operational history.

### Evidence profile

Partial runtime evidence is available. Enforcement logs can evidence operational data handling at evaluation time. Training data provenance requires institutional-layer artefacts, including provider representations, fine-tuning legal basis, and IP clearance records.

## 3. Contract Infrastructure (D3)

What legal architecture governs the deployment?

Contract Infrastructure assesses the legal architecture through which liability is allocated, obligations are defined, and accountability is assigned across the deployment chain. It is directly connected to the four accountability links: design, deployment, operational, and outcome.

### What it assesses

- Customer agreements, including AI-specific provisions, scope limitations, and liability carve-outs.
- Vendor agreements, including model and infrastructure contracts, SLAs, and indemnification structure.
- Data processing agreements and legal basis across deployment chain entities.
- Liability provisions governing AE3 consequences.
- Contractual allocation of accountability across the Decision Supply Chain.
- Political force majeure provisions addressing government supply chain risk designation events as distinct triggers.
- Provider substitution rights with political designation triggers, extending beyond technical API failure scenarios.

#### Item A - Upstream Governance Dependency

**Assessment question**

Do upstream AI provider agreements allocate operational liability to the deploying organisation while withholding governance authority, including audit rights, model change notification, or behavioural guarantees?

**Failure indicators**

- provider liability exclusions for AI outputs
- no audit rights over model behaviour
- unilateral model modification rights
- no notification obligations for material model updates

#### Item B - Policy Governance Dependency

**Assessment question**

Do upstream provider acceptable use policies impose governance constraints on the deploying organisation's autonomous system operations that the organisation cannot contractually resist or anticipate?

**Failure indicators**

- provider discretion to suspend deployment categories
- unclear policy interpretation mechanisms
- no contractual protection against retrospective policy change

### Institutional implication

D3 is a foundational liability dimension. Without adequate contract infrastructure, the organisation has no legal architecture through which to manage adverse autonomous outcomes. The February 2026 Anthropic-Pentagon designation event demonstrated that a government supply chain risk designation can cascade through enterprise customer procurement obligations contractually rather than technically. See [ARAF Assessor Guidance Note GN-002: Cross-Border Deployment Assessment](/assessor-guidance/gn-002/) for cross-border liability architecture assessment methodology.

### Evidence profile

D3 is an institutional-layer dimension. No enforcement log can satisfy D3. It is assessed through legal document review and accountability allocation quality.

## 4. Liability Architecture (D4)

How is liability for the system's autonomous decisions structured, documented, and governed?

Liability Architecture assesses how liability for autonomous action consequences is structured. AE3 outcomes accumulate at operational speed, creating liability exposure that conventional frameworks were not designed to absorb.

### What it assesses

- AE3 liability structure and cap adequacy.
- Carve-out provisions for specific autonomous decision categories.
- Insurance coverage adequacy for AE3 exposure.
- Liability allocation across design, deployment, operational, and outcome links.
- Governance of liability architecture, including review authority, review interval, and trigger events.

### Institutional implication

For insurers, D4 is the coverage architecture dimension. It determines what must be covered, what can be excluded, and how large exposure can become under autonomous decision volume.

### Evidence profile

Enforcement evidence is the precondition, not the structure. Tier 1 enforcement logs demonstrate that controls operated when decisions were made. Independent assessment is still required to determine whether the liability structure itself is adequate.

## 5. Commercial Leverage (D5)

How commercially dependent is the organisation on this system, and how does that dependency constrain governance remediation?

Commercial Leverage assesses the degree to which operational and commercial dependency limits governance remediation options. Where remediation disruption exceeds what the organisation is willing to accept, the governance gap becomes structural rather than contingent.

### What it assesses

- Operational dependency of core processes on the system.
- Revenue concentration linked to system availability and performance.
- Remediation commercial resistance.
- Technology lock-in, portability constraints, and vendor dependency.
- Customer relationship embedding tied to continued system operation.
- Government-adjacent customer exposure, including concentration risk linked to customers with government, defence, or regulated procurement obligations.

### Institutional implication

For investors, D5 is a structural risk signal. A high D5 score with high D3 or D4 indicates a governance deficit the commercial structure is working against correcting.

## 6. Adaptive Stability (D6)

Can governance be maintained as the system and its deployment evolve?

Adaptive Stability assesses the organisation's capacity to maintain governance adequacy over time. A deployment that is governable at initial release may become inadequately governed months later if governance architecture does not evolve with system behaviour.

### What it assesses

- Governance maintenance processes and formal reassessment triggers.
- Change governance over model and rule updates.
- Monitoring architecture and behavioural drift detection.
- Provider designation monitoring for government actions affecting the primary AI provider stack.
- MKP version lineage and rule evolution governance.
- AIOC authority over governance rule-set changes.

#### Infrastructure Dependency Stability

**Assessment question**

Does the organisation rely on upstream AI providers whose service termination or unilateral modification could materially disrupt operational decision systems?

**Evidence required**

- provider contracts
- dependency mapping documentation
- fallback architecture documentation
- operational contingency plan

**Failure indicators**

- no alternative provider capability
- no internal fallback system
- termination clauses permitting immediate suspension
- no documented operational contingency

**Resilience diagnostic**

Can the system continue operating if the primary model provider becomes unavailable for 72 hours?

If the answer is no, the Dimension 6 score increases.

### The Two-Layer Evidentiary Model

D6 evidence adequacy requires two components serving distinct institutional audiences. They are assessed separately and should be maintained separately.

| Component | Records | Primary audience | Evidence tier | What it satisfies |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Runtime enforcement log | MKP identifier governing each decision, enforcement outcome, drift score per evaluation | Insurer or regulator interrogating a specific enforcement decision | Tier 1 if infrastructure-generated and tamper-evident | Rule attribution: which rule set governed this decision at this time |
| MKP registry | Version graph, update authorisation chain, rule change history, change timestamps | Assessor evaluating rule adequacy and evolution over time | Tier 1 if append-only and tamper-evident; Tier 2 if documented institutional record | Rule evolution: how governing rule set changed, when, and who authorised it |

Management representations without supporting runtime or registry evidence constitute Tier 4 evidence and are not admissible for coherence assessment.

Separation is the correct architecture. Decision-level interrogation needs runtime precision. Governance adequacy assessment needs rule-lineage visibility across time.

### The Traversal Path - D6 closing criterion

The runtime log records MKP identifier. The registry records version history for that identifier. The D6 closing criterion is whether a traversal path exists between the two.

| Criterion | Assessment implication |
| --- | --- |
| Runtime log records MKP identifier per decision | Verifiable from enforcement log. Required for rule attribution. |
| MKP registry exists and is maintained | Assessor verifies access, tamper-evidence, and append-only architecture. |
| Traversal path from MKP identifier to version graph | Closing criterion. If absent, records exist in isolation and rule lineage cannot be reconstructed from enforcement evidence alone. |

A deterministic system enforcing the wrong rules is deterministically wrong. Rule attribution proves that rules were applied. Independent assessment determines whether those rules were adequate for the risk profile.

### Institutional implication

For insurers, D6 is the ongoing governance maintenance signal across the coverage period. A high D6 score indicates that governance posture at policy inception may not match governance posture at claim time.

## Compound Risk

### Multiplier logic

The six dimensions are not independent. Weaknesses compound rather than merely add. Individual weaknesses may be manageable. Compound weaknesses create systemic exposure by reducing remediation options and increasing remediation cost.

| Multiplier | Trigger | Compound exposure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Systemic Escalation | D1 >= 4 and D4 >= 4 | High autonomy with inadequate liability architecture. AE3 volume is high and liability framework is not designed to absorb consequences. Most severe compound exposure. |
| Infrastructure Collapse | D3 >= 4 and D1 >= 3 | Significant autonomy with inadequate contract infrastructure. Liability allocation at execution boundary is contractually ungoverned. |
| Infrastructure Collapse | D3 ≥ 4 AND D1 ≥ 3 | Infrastructure Collapse occurs when contractual governance infrastructure is inadequate relative to the level of autonomous action the system is authorised to take. Trigger conditions include: (A) absence of documented decision authority allocation across the supply chain; (B) contractual terms that do not address autonomous decision consequences; absence of liability caps or indemnification provisions calibrated to AE3 exposure; absence of escalation and override provisions; and absence of D6 Infrastructure Dependency Stability documentation. Trigger: D3 ≥ 4 AND D1 ≥ 3. |
| Leverage Collapse | D5 >= 4 and D4 >= 3 | High commercial dependency with inadequate liability architecture. System becomes structurally resistant to remediation. |
| Political Cascade | D5 >= 4 and D3 >= 3 with single-provider dependency and government-adjacent customer concentration | High commercial dependency combined with inadequate political force majeure or provider substitution provisions governing government designation events. |
| Probabilistic Cascade | D1 ≥ 4 AND D6 ≥ 4 | Probabilistic Cascade occurs when autonomous system behaviour can evolve faster than the governance architecture responsible for controlling it. In these conditions, the governance posture assessed at deployment may not reflect the governance posture operating when consequential decisions occur. |

Infrastructure Collapse applies where autonomous decision systems depend on infrastructure whose availability, behaviour, or governance cannot be reliably controlled by the deploying organisation.

This includes dependencies created through contractual arrangements with upstream AI providers whose termination rights, policy authority, or unilateral modification powers could materially disrupt system operation or remove governance authority from the deploying organisation.

### Infrastructure Collapse (+2) Trigger Conditions

An underwriter assessing a system with Infrastructure Collapse active faces a deployment where autonomous action volume is not supported by the contractual architecture required to allocate, cap, or transfer the liability that volume creates.

Infrastructure Collapse (+2) is triggered when one or more of the following conditions are present:

1. Upstream governance dependency  
Critical AI infrastructure is controlled by an external provider whose contractual rights exceed the governance authority of the deploying organisation.

2. Termination dependency  
A provider can suspend or terminate access to infrastructure required for system operation.

3. Policy governance dependency  
Upstream acceptable use policies allow the provider to veto or suspend deployment categories operated by the deploying organisation.

4. Unilateral model modification risk  
The provider may change model behaviour, training, or safety parameters without the deploying organisation's governance approval or prior notice.

5. Single-provider infrastructure concentration  
No operational fallback capability exists if the primary provider becomes unavailable.

GBI scoring direction is fixed: higher numbers represent greater governance risk, not stronger performance.

## Institutional Use

The dimensional profile serves each audience differently.

### For Boards

Identifies which governance questions require immediate attention and which dimensions are producing multiplier exposure the board must address.

### For Insurers

D4 weakness determines AE3 coverage requirements. D1 weakness determines commitment authority exposure. D6 weakness determines ongoing monitoring conditions that should be required through the coverage period.

### For Investors

Distinguishes structural risks (D3 and D4, requiring contractual and architectural remediation) from operational risks (D1 and D6, responding to process and monitoring improvements). This distinction governs remediation timelines and cost expectations.

### For Regulators

Provides a comparable, independently assessed governance posture classification across supervised populations, aligned with EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, and APRA CPS 230.
