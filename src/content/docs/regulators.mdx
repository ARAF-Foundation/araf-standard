  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">01</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">The Accountability Gap</h3>
    <p className="body-text">Regulatory frameworks are designed to ensure that organisations remain accountable for the consequences of their actions. Autonomous systems alter the accountability structure those frameworks assume.</p>
    <p className="body-text">Traditional accountability mechanisms assume that consequential decisions have identifiable human authors whose authority, knowledge, and conduct can be examined when outcomes are questioned. Autonomous systems may produce decisions through machine learning infrastructure, data pipelines, human review processes, and execution environments operating across organisational and jurisdictional boundaries.</p>
    <p className="body-text">When an adverse outcome occurs, regulators must determine how the decision was produced, whether the organisation exercised appropriate governance, whether it had visibility into system behaviour, and whether it maintained records sufficient to demonstrate accountability. The questions regulators apply in these circumstances are the same questions the ARAF framework is designed to make answerable: <em>Who was responsible? What did they know? What did they do?</em></p>
    <p className="body-text">In many deployments, these questions cannot be answered from contemporaneous governance records. The governance architectures that once made institutional decision-making traceable were designed for human-authored decisions, not distributed computational systems operating at machine scale.</p>
    <div className="callout">
      ARAF provides governance infrastructure that enables autonomous decisions to remain reconstructable for supervisory purposes. The framework is designed to complement existing regulatory regimes, not to replace them or to limit regulatory authority.
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">02</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">Governance Outputs Relevant to Supervision</h3>
    <p className="body-text">An ARAF assessment produces five outputs relevant to regulatory supervision.</p>
    <ul className="araf-list">
      <li><strong>Dimensional governance profile</strong> - governance posture across six dimensions, identifying where accountability gaps exist within the deployment's operational architecture</li>
      <li><strong>Governance Benchmark Index (GBI)</strong> - composite governance score on a 1.0 to 5.0 scale; lower scores indicate stronger governance architecture and stronger supervisory visibility</li>
      <li><strong>Multiplier analysis</strong> - structural governance risks where dimensional weaknesses compound, potentially creating systemic accountability gaps</li>
      <li><strong>Evidence quality assessment</strong> - whether governance records are infrastructure-generated (Tier 1), contemporaneously documented (Tier 2), or retrospectively reconstructed (Tier 3), and the supervisory confidence each tier warrants</li>
      <li><strong>Certification tier</strong> - the designation based on the GBI score, indicating whether the governance posture meets defined institutional thresholds</li>
    </ul>

    <p className="body-text">ARAF defines three certification tiers. The GBI provides a comparable governance signal that regulators can evaluate across supervised entities without requiring each entity to describe its AI governance in bespoke, non-comparable terms.</p>
    <div className="needs-grid">
      <div className="needs-cell">
        <p className="needs-label">ARAF Assessed</p>
        <p className="needs-desc"><strong>Independent evaluation completed.</strong> Governance posture has been independently evaluated by an accredited assessor. No minimum GBI score required. Entry level for supervisory governance discussions.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="needs-cell">
        <p className="needs-label">ARAF Compliant - GBI &le; 2.50</p>
        <p className="needs-desc"><strong>Minimum institutional threshold met.</strong> Governance posture supports standard insurance coverage terms, enterprise procurement requirements, and the governance threshold that supervisory expectations for regulated activities typically require.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="needs-cell">
        <p className="needs-label">ARAF Certified - GBI &le; 1.75</p>
        <p className="needs-desc"><strong>Full agentic bankability conditions met.</strong> Governance posture supports classification, insurance, financing, and the highest level of institutional reliance. Governance architecture can be demonstrated from contemporaneous records across all six dimensions.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">03</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">Reconstructability and Supervision</h3>
    <p className="body-text">Regulatory supervision requires the ability to reconstruct consequential decisions when outcomes are challenged. ARAF evaluates whether organisations maintain governance records capable of supporting this reconstruction. The framework distinguishes three evidence quality tiers.</p>
    <div className="needs-grid">
      <div className="needs-cell">
        <p className="needs-label">Tier 1</p>
        <p className="needs-desc"><strong>Infrastructure-generated.</strong> Records produced automatically by governance infrastructure at the time governance is exercised: scope boundary enforcement, commitment authority checks, action admission decisions. Produced in a form the organisation cannot alter retroactively. Most reliable for supervisory purposes.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="needs-cell">
        <p className="needs-label">Tier 2</p>
        <p className="needs-desc"><strong>Contemporaneous documentation.</strong> Records created at the time governance decisions were made: board papers, oversight committee minutes, deployment approval records, anomaly escalation records. Not produced in response to subsequent investigation.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="needs-cell">
        <p className="needs-label">Tier 3</p>
        <p className="needs-desc"><strong>Reconstructed documentation.</strong> Evidence assembled after the fact from existing materials. Subject to selection and framing choices. Carries significantly lower evidential weight for supervisory purposes.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p className="body-text">An organisation's ability to produce Tier 1 and Tier 2 evidence is the operational definition of supervisory visibility into autonomous decision-making. ARAF assessments evaluate evidence quality as a core component of governance posture.</p>
    <div className="callout">
      An organisation that holds ARAF Certified or ARAF Compliant status holds contemporaneous, independently produced evidence that its governance architecture was assessed against a defined standard and found adequate at the time of assessment. That evidence does not constitute a statutory safe harbour. It is, however, the kind of contemporaneous evidence that regulators conducting supervisory review will find relevant to whether reasonable precautions were taken.
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">04</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">Assessment Credibility</h3>
    <p className="body-text">ARAF certifications are issued by independent, accredited assessors. The Assessor Accreditation Standard establishes four requirements: demonstrated qualification in governance assessment and ARAF methodology; structural independence from the organisation being assessed; methodology adherence (assessors apply the published methodology without modification); and professional accountability, including submission to quality assurance review and accreditation withdrawal for sustained quality failure.</p>
    <p className="body-text">The accreditation architecture was designed with specific reference to the failure mode documented in the FTC's 2014 action against TRUSTe, where inadequate assessor oversight produced over 1,000 certifications that did not reflect actual practice. ARAF addresses that failure directly: assessors are subject to accountability review, and accreditation is contingent on maintaining assessment quality.</p>
    <p className="body-text">The assessment methodology is open (licensed under CC BY 4.0), making the assessment process independently reviewable by any regulatory body that considers referencing the framework in supervisory expectations. Self-certification does not satisfy the ARAF independence requirement. Self-reported governance is evidence of intent. Independent assessment is evidence of fact.</p>
  </div>

      <div className="trigger-item">

        <p className="trigger-head">Periodic supervisory review</p>
        <p className="trigger-body">Governance reviews examining how organisations supervise autonomous systems operating within regulated activities. The GBI provides a comparable signal across supervised entities.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="trigger-item">
        <p className="trigger-head">Incident investigation</p>
        <p className="trigger-body">Following adverse outcomes, requiring organisations to demonstrate how decisions were produced and governed from contemporaneous records. The assessment report and evidence quality tier classification provide the structured evidence base.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="trigger-item">
        <p className="trigger-head">Policy development</p>
        <p className="trigger-body">Evaluating governance infrastructure across industries to understand how autonomous systems are being governed in practice, using the dimensional profile as a comparable governance signal across entities.</p>
      </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">06</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">Incorporating ARAF into Supervisory Practice</h3>
    <p className="body-text">Regulators may incorporate ARAF governance signals into supervisory practice through several mechanisms.</p>
    <ul className="araf-list">
      <li><strong>Supervisory expectations</strong> - referencing ARAF certification tiers in supervisory guidance for entities deploying autonomous systems in regulated activities, establishing governance thresholds that supervised entities are expected to meet</li>
      <li><strong>Cross-entity comparison</strong> - using the GBI as a comparable governance signal across supervised entities, replacing the current condition in which each organisation describes its AI governance in terms that resist comparison</li>
      <li><strong>Structured evidence in supervisory reviews</strong> - accepting ARAF assessment reports (dimensional profiles, multiplier analysis, evidence quality tier classification) as structured governance evidence during periodic examinations, reducing the supervisory burden of conducting bespoke governance investigations for each entity</li>
      <li><strong>Licensing conditions</strong> - requiring ARAF assessment (at a specified minimum certification tier) as a condition for entities seeking to deploy autonomous systems at material scale in regulated activities</li>
      <li><strong>Targeted supervisory attention</strong> - using dimensional profiles to identify entities with concentrated governance weaknesses (particularly multiplier conditions) that warrant closer supervisory review</li>
    </ul>
    <p className="body-text">These mechanisms allow regulators to incorporate governance signals into existing supervisory processes without building a bespoke AI governance assessment capability.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">07</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">Decision Supply Chain Visibility</h3>
    <p className="body-text">Autonomous systems often operate within distributed decision infrastructure involving multiple organisations. The Decision Supply Chain may include model providers, data providers, fine-tuning organisations, outsourced review teams, and infrastructure providers, often spanning multiple jurisdictions.</p>
    <p className="body-text">ARAF assessments require organisations to map the Decision Supply Chain through which autonomous decisions are produced and to identify how governance obligations are allocated across participating entities. This mapping identifies where accountability resides when decisions are produced through distributed infrastructure.</p>
    <p className="body-text">The deploying organisation at the centre of the chain retains accountability for the chain's outputs regardless of where individual links operate. Jurisdictional complexity does not distribute accountability; it multiplies governance risk if the contractual and governance architecture has not addressed it.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">08</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">Relationship to Existing Regulatory Frameworks</h3>
    <p className="body-text">ARAF is governance infrastructure. It is designed to complement existing regulatory regimes by providing structured governance signals relevant to supervisory accountability.</p>
    <div className="needs-grid">
      <div className="needs-cell">
        <p className="needs-label">Financial services</p>
        <p className="needs-desc"><strong>Prudential and conduct obligations.</strong> ARAF dimensional assessments map to governance obligations under prudential frameworks (APRA CPS 230 operational risk and service provider governance), conduct frameworks (ASIC regulatory expectations for AI governance), and director duty of care obligations (s 180(1) Corporations Act). Dimension 1 (Autonomy Gradient) and Dimension 4 (Liability Architecture) address accountability architecture directly relevant to supervisory review.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="needs-cell">
        <p className="needs-label">Operational resilience</p>
        <p className="needs-desc"><strong>Third-party and operational risk.</strong> The Decision Supply Chain assessment addresses third-party governance requirements under APRA CPS 230 and equivalent frameworks. Dimension 3 (Contract Infrastructure) evaluates whether vendor and service provider agreements reflect the governance obligations that autonomous decision infrastructure creates.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="needs-cell">
        <p className="needs-label">AI governance</p>
        <p className="needs-desc"><strong>EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001.</strong> ARAF operates as the governance architecture assessment layer above process compliance frameworks such as ISO 42001 and risk methodology frameworks such as the NIST AI RMF. It translates their outputs into institutional governance signals that supervisors can interpret and compare across entities.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p className="body-text">Certification under ARAF does not constitute regulatory approval or compliance with any statutory framework. It provides governance information that regulators may consider alongside other supervisory tools. The framework does not limit regulatory authority; it provides structured governance documentation that makes that authority easier to exercise.</p>
  </div>
