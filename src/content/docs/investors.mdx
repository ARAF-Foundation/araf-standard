      <h2>ARAF for Investors</h2>
      <p className="hero-lede">Governance is a financial variable. Autonomous systems, systems capable of producing consequential decisions without real-time human authorship, that lack governance architecture create operational, regulatory, and institutional risks that affect capital. The Agentic Risk Architecture Framework (ARAF) provides a structured signal for evaluating <a href="/glossary/#governance-posture">governance posture</a> across the investment lifecycle.</p>
    </div>

        <p className="problem-label">The capital governance problem</p>
        <p className="problem-statement">The investment question is not whether the autonomous system performs. It is whether the organisation has constructed governance infrastructure capable of demonstrating how autonomous decisions are produced, governed, and reconstructed from contemporaneous records when those decisions are examined.</p>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">01</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">Governance as Investment Risk</h3>
    <p className="body-text">When a business deploys autonomous systems capable of producing consequential decisions, credit approvals, underwriting determinations, pricing decisions, fraud detection, compliance monitoring, operational automation, the governance structure surrounding those systems becomes part of the investment risk profile.</p>
    <p className="body-text">These decisions are not produced by a single model in isolation. They travel through a <a href="/decision-supply-chain/">Decision Supply Chain</a>: the sequence of data inputs, model processing, decision formation, execution infrastructure, and institutional exposure through which a consequential decision is produced. Where governance posture across that chain cannot be evaluated, capital exposure becomes difficult to assess.</p>
    <div className="callout">
      <strong>The governance gap in current markets.</strong> Most autonomous system deployments can demonstrate technical performance. Far fewer can demonstrate governance architecture: the documented accountability structures, evidence records, and <a href="/glossary/#reconstructability">reconstructability</a>, the ability to demonstrate how consequential decisions were produced from contemporaneous governance records, that allow governance to be demonstrated when it is examined.
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">02</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">How Governance Posture Affects Investment Risk</h3>
    <div className="risk-grid">
      <div className="risk-cell">
        <p className="risk-label">Regulatory exposure</p>
        <p className="risk-desc">Autonomous systems without governance documentation may create enforcement exposure when the organisation cannot demonstrate from contemporaneous records who was responsible for the system's decisions, what they knew, and what they did.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="risk-cell">
        <p className="risk-label">Litigation risk</p>
        <p className="risk-desc">Where consequential decisions cannot be reconstructed from governance records, defending claims related to discrimination, negligence, or operational failure becomes materially more difficult and more expensive.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="risk-cell">
        <p className="risk-label">Operational fragility</p>
        <p className="risk-desc">Systems without clear accountability architecture may experience governance failures as models are retrained, data sources change, or deployment contexts shift. Unresolved governance obligations compound over time: the longer a system operates without adequate governance infrastructure, the more expensive remediation becomes.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="risk-cell">
        <p className="risk-label">Institutional reliance</p>
        <p className="risk-desc">Enterprise customers, insurers, and regulators increasingly require governance documentation before entering institutional relationships. Weak governance posture may limit market access regardless of product capability.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">03</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">The Governance Signal Investors Receive</h3>
    <p className="body-text">An ARAF assessment produces five outputs relevant to capital allocation.</p>
    <ul className="araf-list">
      <li><strong>Dimensional governance profile</strong> - governance posture scored across six dimensions, identifying where exposure exists within the system's operational architecture</li>
      <li><strong>Governance Benchmark Index (GBI)</strong> - composite governance signal on a scale from 1.0 to 5.0; lower scores represent stronger governance posture</li>
      <li><strong>Multiplier analysis</strong> - identification of structural governance risks where dimensional weaknesses compound beyond what a simple average would indicate</li>
      <li><strong>Evidence quality evaluation</strong> - assessment of whether governance records can support reconstruction of consequential decisions from contemporaneous evidence</li>
      <li><strong>Certification tier</strong> - the designation based on the GBI score, indicating whether the governance posture meets defined institutional thresholds (ARAF Assessed, ARAF Compliant at GBI &le; 2.50, ARAF Certified at GBI &le; 1.75)</li>
    </ul>
    <p className="body-text">These outputs allow investors to evaluate governance posture using a structured classification signal rather than relying solely on narrative disclosures from management.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">04</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">Interpreting GBI Scores</h3>
    <p className="body-text">The GBI functions as a governance classification signal rather than a technical performance metric. The scale is intentionally inverted: a score of 1.0 represents the strongest governance posture; a score of 5.0 represents critical governance exposure. This inversion mirrors the logic of credit ratings and makes the score immediately legible to institutional audiences. Certification thresholds provide a reference interpretation.</p>
    <table className="gbi-table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Certification Tier</th><th>GBI Threshold</th><th>Investor Interpretation</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr><td className="tier">ARAF Certified</td><td>&le; 1.75</td><td>Governance architecture supports the full conditions of institutional reliance: the system can be classified, governed, insured, financed, and relied upon</td></tr>
        <tr><td className="tier">ARAF Compliant</td><td>&le; 2.50</td><td>Governance posture meets the minimum institutional threshold for reliance in most deployment contexts</td></tr>
        <tr><td className="tier">ARAF Assessed</td><td>Any score</td><td>Governance posture has been independently evaluated against a defined standard; baseline established for remediation planning</td></tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p className="body-text">A GBI composite score above 2.50 represents a remediation obligation that an investor should model as a post-investment cost. A score at or below 1.75 represents a governance asset that supports premium valuation and institutional adoption. The dimensional profile identifies which risks are structural (Dimension 3 and Dimension 4, requiring contractual and architectural change with longer remediation timelines) and which are operational (Dimension 1 and Dimension 6, responding to process and monitoring improvements within normal investment timelines).</p>
    <p className="body-text">A GBI score above 2.50 indicates a governance posture that requires remediation before institutional reliance is appropriate. The dimensional profile accompanying the GBI score identifies which specific governance dimensions require attention.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">05</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">Agentic Bankability: The Investment Threshold</h3>
    <p className="body-text"><a href="/trust-architecture/#agentic-bankability">Agentic bankability</a> is the condition in which an autonomous system can be classified, governed, insured, financed, and relied upon by the institutions that must assume responsibility for its behaviour. The term is derived from the project finance concept of bankability: the condition under which an asset meets the governance standards required for institutional financing.</p>
    <p className="body-text">Agentic bankability is satisfied when four conditions are met:</p>
    <ul className="araf-list">
      <li><strong>Identifiability</strong> - the system and the decisions it makes can be described with sufficient precision to allow governance to be assigned</li>
      <li><strong>Measurability</strong> - the system's governance posture can be assessed against a defined standard</li>
      <li><strong>Governability</strong> - the system's behaviour can be governed through documented accountability structures</li>
      <li><strong>Insurability</strong> - the system's risk exposure can be underwritten by institutional counterparties</li>
    </ul>
    <p className="body-text">These four conditions map directly to the governance questions investors face at every stage of the investment lifecycle. ARAF certification is the structured signal that indicates whether, and to what degree, these conditions have been met.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">06</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">Investment Decision Triggers</h3>
    <div className="trigger-grid">
      <div className="trigger-item">
        <p className="trigger-head">Pre-investment diligence</p>
        <p className="trigger-body">Evaluating governance architecture before committing capital to a company whose products or operations rely on autonomous systems. The dimensional profile identifies specific governance exposures that may not be visible in technical due diligence.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="trigger-item">
        <p className="trigger-head">Growth-stage governance review</p>
        <p className="trigger-body">As companies scale their use of autonomous systems, governance architecture becomes increasingly relevant to operational stability. Governance obligations that are deferred accumulate as governance debt: the longer a system operates without adequate governance infrastructure, the more expensive remediation becomes.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="trigger-item">
        <p className="trigger-head">Enterprise sales readiness</p>
        <p className="trigger-body">Companies seeking to sell to enterprise customers encounter governance requirements in procurement and compliance reviews. ARAF certification provides a structured governance signal that enterprise procurement and compliance teams can evaluate against their requirements.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="trigger-item">
        <p className="trigger-head">Exit preparation</p>
        <p className="trigger-body">Governance posture may affect regulatory diligence, insurance coverage, and buyer confidence during acquisition or public listing processes. A current ARAF assessment provides acquirers and underwriters with an independent governance evaluation rather than management representations alone.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">07</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">How Different Investor Groups Use ARAF</h3>
    <p className="body-text">The governance signal is consistent across investor types. The stage at which it matters in the investment lifecycle, and the specific assessment outputs most relevant, differ.</p>
    <div className="investor-grid">
      <div className="investor-cell">
        <p className="investor-type">Venture Capital</p>
        <p className="investor-focus">Portfolio governance diagnostic</p>
        <p className="investor-use">Evaluating whether governance architecture will allow the company to scale without creating regulatory or operational fragility. The dimensional profile and multiplier analysis identify governance gaps that would compound as the system scales.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="investor-cell">
        <p className="investor-type">Private Equity and Growth</p>
        <p className="investor-focus">Risk management infrastructure</p>
        <p className="investor-use">Governance posture as part of operational risk management and integration planning. The GBI composite score provides a comparable signal across portfolio companies; the remediation roadmap supports post-investment governance improvement.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="investor-cell">
        <p className="investor-type">Institutional Asset Owners</p>
        <p className="investor-focus">Systemic governance assurance</p>
        <p className="investor-use">Governance posture assessed in the context of fiduciary responsibility and responsible investment mandates. The certification tier provides an independent governance classification that can be incorporated into portfolio-level risk reporting.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="investor-cell">
        <p className="investor-type">Angels and Accelerators</p>
        <p className="investor-focus">Founder governance signal</p>
        <p className="investor-use">Whether founders have structured governance capable of supporting future institutional adoption. An ARAF Assessed designation at early stage establishes the governance baseline from which the company can mature toward certification.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="investor-cell">
        <p className="investor-type">Government and Public Investors</p>
        <p className="investor-focus">Policy and accountability lens</p>
        <p className="investor-use">Governance posture evaluated through regulatory accountability and responsible innovation criteria. The evidence quality evaluation confirms whether governance claims are supported by contemporaneous records or retrospective reconstruction.</p>
      </div>
      <div className="investor-cell investor-cell--summary">
        <p className="investor-type">The common question</p>
        <p className="investor-focus">Across all investor types, the underlying question is the same: has this autonomous system achieved agentic bankability - can it be classified, governed, insured, financed, and relied upon by the institutions that must assume responsibility for its behaviour?</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="content-section">
    <p className="section-num">08</p>
    <h3 className="section-title">Assessment Credibility</h3>
    <p className="body-text">ARAF certifications are issued by independent, accredited assessors operating under a published methodology with defined qualification, independence, and accountability requirements. The methodology is open (licensed under CC BY 4.0), making the assessment process independently reviewable by any investor conducting diligence.</p>
    <p className="body-text">Self-assessment does not satisfy the ARAF independence requirement. Self-reported governance is evidence of intent. Independent assessment is evidence of fact. When an accredited assessor issues a certification, it accepts professional responsibility for the quality of the assessment.</p>
    <p className="body-text">A company whose autonomous systems hold ARAF Compliant or ARAF Certified status is positioned for standard insurance coverage terms. A company that cannot produce a governance signal faces coverage exclusions that create unquantifiable risk for investors. Governance posture affects insurability, and insurability affects the risk profile of the investment.</p>
  </div>
