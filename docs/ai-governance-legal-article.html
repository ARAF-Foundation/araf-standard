AI Governance for In-House Legal Teams: A Practical Operating Model for the World We Actually Live In

In-house legal teams do not need a perfect AI governance framework before acting. They need an operating model that can identify live usage, classify risk, and make adoption governable now.

Carly Martin Founder, Institute for Autonomous Governance

The Question In-House Teams Are Really Asking

A GC recently posted on LinkedIn: does anyone have practical, actionable guidance on AI governance for in-house legal teams? Not a vendor pitch. Not a policy that sits on a shelf. Something that reflects the reality that AI is already being used across the organisation, whether legal formally approved it or not.

The comments made the problem clear. This field does not feel settled. Existing frameworks often do not match operational reality, yet in-house teams are being asked to govern live adoption while the rules, tools, and risk patterns keep changing.

What follows is not a complete answer to AI governance. It is a practical operating model for in-house legal teams trying to govern AI use in organisations where adoption is already ahead of policy.

80% of employees use AI in their roles; only 22% use exclusively employer-provided tools
57% of employees actively hide their AI usage at work
7% of organisations have a documented AI governance framework that is actively followed

Why This Field Still Feels Unsolved

AI governance for in-house legal is not a solved discipline, and pretending otherwise is part of the problem. But organisations do not get to defer the issue until a perfect framework appears.

The practical task for legal is narrower and harder: identify where AI is already being used, distinguish low-consequence productivity use from high-consequence decision use, and put in place controls that make adoption governable rather than invisible.

Employee AI use is now widespread, and a large proportion of that use still occurs outside employer-provided environments. The term "shadow AI" is counterproductive. It implies employees are doing something wrong by using tools that make them more productive. A more useful framing: you have ungoverned AI. The goal is visibility, controlled pathways, and accountable use, not blanket prohibition.

Research into governance failure identifies three root causes: misaligned goals, unclear ownership, and unchanged workflows. A static policy document addresses none of these. Policies are necessary, but insufficient on their own. They tell people what they cannot do without creating a structure for what they can do, under what conditions, with what oversight. Governance needs to be operational, adaptive, and able to account for developments such as agents, connectors, and increasing model capability.

The Four Things Giving In-House Teams Immediate Heartburn

Four specific pressure points are driving the sense that existing governance artefacts are lagging operational reality.

Legal exposure

1. Privilege instability after Heppner

In February 2026, Judge Rakoff held in United States v. Heppner that a defendant's communications with Anthropic's consumer Claude platform were protected by neither attorney-client privilege nor work product doctrine. The court found no privileged relationship, no reasonable expectation of confidentiality, and that sharing AI outputs with lawyers afterward did not "alchemically change" them into privileged communications.

In-house teams should treat unmanaged consumer AI tools as inappropriate for privileged, confidential, or trade secret material. Enterprise platforms with negotiated ZDR and contractual training prohibitions may satisfy Kovel requirements, but this remains untested in court.

The same principle extends to autonomous decision records. Heppner's logic is that governance cannot be asserted after the fact; it must be demonstrable from records produced independently of the system being assessed. A log the system generated about its own actions does not establish institutional admissibility.[17]

Operational risk

2. Agents going off-script

Recent incidents have shown that autonomous systems can expose restricted information, ignore operational constraints, or continue acting after instructions were meant to stop them. In one case, an AI agent autonomously exposed proprietary code and business strategies to unauthorised users for approximately two hours. In another, an agent deleted hundreds of messages after an automated process stripped its safety instructions during context management.

Nearly half of CISOs surveyed in 2026 had observed AI agents exhibiting unintended or unauthorised behaviour, and only 5% were confident they could contain a compromised AI agent.[9]

Infrastructure risk

3. Connectors, MCP, and permission sprawl

Model Context Protocol (MCP) gives AI agents standardised access to external tools and enterprise systems. Once a tool is connected, it is controlled by the model, with no human operating the lever. If an MCP-connected model sends an erroneous notice to a counterparty, submits a regulatory filing with incorrect data, or modifies a contract record, the legal consequences flow back to the organisation.

Because MCP connectors can chain together, errors propagate across multiple systems before they are caught. The more autonomy built into the workflow, the wider the potential blast radius of a single error.[11]

Contractual risk

4. Opaque training data and vendor uncertainty

Most organisations cannot confirm what data their AI vendors trained on, whether their inputs are being used to improve models, or what happens to their data if the vendor becomes subject to a litigation hold. In 2025, a US court ordered a major AI provider to preserve all user interactions as part of a copyright lawsuit. Only customers with enterprise or Zero Data Retention agreements were excluded.

Vendors also add AI capabilities mid-contract, creating gaps where existing terms no longer address the actual risks.[13]

A Practical Six-Step Operating Model

The consensus among practitioners is to build on what you already have (privacy, vendor risk, data governance) rather than creating AI governance from scratch, and to layer in AI-specific controls where existing processes fall short.

Map what is actually happening

Before writing or rewriting policy, inventory every AI tool in use, including personal subscriptions and MCP connectors. You cannot govern use you cannot see. Cross-reference IT procurement records, expense reports, and network logs.

Classify by risk, not by tool

Not every AI use case carries the same risk. Marketing copy and autonomous underwriting are fundamentally different governance conversations. Use a tiered classification that drives proportional controls: standard, elevated, high, and autonomous.

Secure the data layer

For any deployment handling sensitive, confidential, or privileged information, require Zero Data Retention configurations. ZDR has two components: a contractual commitment in the vendor agreement and a technical implementation through the relevant API configuration. One without the other is insufficient.

Create approved pathways

Employees will continue using AI whether or not formal approval catches up. The governance objective is to move usage into visible, managed pathways rather than forcing it into unmanaged ones. For each risk tier, define what tools are approved, what data can be input, what human review is required, and how outputs are documented.

Toolkit: Annexure D, Acceptable Use Framework

Address agents and connectors specifically

Traditional AI governance assumes a human types a prompt and reviews an output. Agents and MCP connectors break that model. For autonomous deployments, require: agent identity verification, short-lived scoped credentials, policy gates on every tool call, sandboxed execution, approval workflows for sensitive actions, and complete action lineage logging.

Logging alone is insufficient. For each consequential autonomous decision, the record must establish four things: what the system did and when (Execution Event); what state it acted on, captured at execution time by independent infrastructure and not reconstructed afterward (Contextual State); who or what authorised the action (Authority and Attribution); and a tamper-evident integrity anchor produced independently of the system that generated the record. A decision log missing any of these components does not establish reconstructability. It establishes that something happened. That is not the same thing.[17]

EIS-01 — Canonical governance decision record: four required components
C1
Execution Event

What the system did, when, and which system instance produced it. The primary event record.

C2
Contextual State

Input data state at execution, model version, policy set invoked. Must be captured contemporaneously — not reconstructed after the fact.

C3
Authority & Attribution

Who or what authorised the action. Links the decision to the governance architecture under which it was produced.

C4
Integrity Anchor

Tamper-evident reference produced by infrastructure independent of the system. Without C4, the record is at most Tier 2 evidence regardless of C1–C3 quality.

A record missing any component does not establish reconstructability.  ·  Source: EIS-01 Evidence Infrastructure Standard, Institute for Autonomous Governance (March 2026)

Align to a framework and build measurable governance

For teams that need a more formal governance classification layer, frameworks such as NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, the EU AI Act, or ARAF's Governance Benchmark Index can provide structure. The question is not which framework name appears on the slide deck. The question is whether your organisation can classify use cases, allocate accountability, control high-consequence deployments, and evidence governance when challenged.

Framework Primary strength Key limitation
NIST AI RMF Flexible, sector-agnostic. Strong risk-based foundation. Widely adopted as baseline. Voluntary. No certification pathway. Does not produce externally verifiable governance signals.
ISO 42001 First certifiable AI management system standard. Third-party verifiable. Useful for procurement and regulatory purposes. Does not yet address autonomous agent governance specifically. Certification is resource-intensive.
EU AI Act Compliance ceiling for EU operations. Risk-based classification with defined conformity assessment for high-risk systems. Jurisdiction-limited. Compliance framing rather than governance architecture. Limited agent-specific provisions.
ARAF / GBI Designed specifically for autonomous system deployments. Produces measurable GBI score for boards, insurers, and institutional stakeholders. Open standard (CC BY 4.0). Newer standard. Accredited assessor ecosystem still building. Requires institutional familiarity to interpret.

Practitioner consensus: use NIST as the risk-based foundation, ISO 42001 where certifiable governance is required, EU AI Act as the compliance ceiling for EU operations, and ARAF/GBI where measurable governance signals are needed for institutional stakeholders.

Legal as Both Governed Function and Governance Function

The legal team needs to be using AI itself to credibly advise the organisation. A legal department that prohibits AI internally while advising business units on AI governance lacks both the practical understanding and the institutional credibility to lead.

Internal credibility matters here. A legal team that has no governed internal AI use of its own will struggle to set realistic rules for the rest of the enterprise.

That means the legal team's own AI adoption, with appropriate guardrails, humans in the loop, and enterprise-grade tools, is a governance accelerant, not a governance risk.

"Rather than viewing governance as a compliance exercise or a brake on innovation, leading organisations will recognise it as an accelerator. With clear guardrails, defined ownership, and transparent evaluation processes, teams can move faster and avoid costly missteps."

ValidMind, 2026

What a Legal Team Can Do in the Next 90 Days

None of these steps require a perfect framework. All of them make your organisation materially more governable than it was 90 days ago.

90-Day Action List
  1. Map actual usage across the organisation, including personal subscriptions and unapproved tools.
  2. Define no-go zones for privileged, confidential, and highly sensitive material.
  3. Classify your top use cases by consequence, not by vendor name.
  4. Create approved pathways for low-risk and high-consequence use.
  5. Review vendor terms for retention, training, permissions, and connector access.
  6. Establish an escalation path for agents and autonomous actions, including a kill switch and incident protocol.

AI governance for in-house legal is not yet a settled discipline. But that is not a reason to wait. The organisations that will handle this transition best will not be those with the longest policies or the loudest AI strategy. They will be the ones that can see where AI is being used, distinguish low-consequence productivity use from high-consequence decision use, and build controls that make adoption governable in practice.

The legal team that answers that question with a system, not a document, is the one that will define how governed AI adoption works in practice.
